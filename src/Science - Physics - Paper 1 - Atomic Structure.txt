Basic	What is radioactive decay and what are the three types?	Unstable nuclei emit radiation spontaneously to become more stable. Alpha (α): 2 protons + 2 neutrons (helium nucleus) — most ionising, least penetrating (stopped by paper/skin). Beta (β): high-speed electron from nucleus — moderately ionising (stopped by aluminium). Gamma (γ): electromagnetic radiation — least ionising, most penetrating (reduced by thick lead/concrete).	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
Cloze	Alpha decay: atomic number decreases by {{c1::2}}, mass number decreases by {{c2::4}}. Beta decay: atomic number increases by {{c3::1}}, mass number stays the {{c4::same}} (a neutron becomes a proton + electron).		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
Cloze	Half-life: time taken for {{c1::half}} the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. After 2 half-lives: {{c2::25%}} remains. After 3 half-lives: {{c3::12.5%}} remains. Used for {{c4::radioactive dating}} of rocks and organic material.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
Basic	What are the uses of nuclear radiation?	Medical: gamma used in radiotherapy (kills cancer cells), beta used in tracers. Industrial: gamma used to check for cracks in pipes/metal. Carbon-14 dating (archaeological). Nuclear power: fission of uranium-235 releases energy. Nuclear fusion (stars, potential future energy): joins nuclei together, releases enormous energy. Basic	What is nuclear fission and how does a chain reaction occur?	<b>Nuclear fission:</b> a large, unstable nucleus (e.g. uranium-235 or plutonium-239) <b>absorbs a slow-moving neutron</b> → splits into two smaller nuclei + 2–3 neutrons + large amount of energy (kinetic energy of fragments + gamma radiation).<br><b>Chain reaction:</b> the neutrons released go on to cause further fissions → exponentially increasing reactions. In a <b>nuclear reactor</b>, control rods (boron) absorb excess neutrons to control the rate. The <b>moderator</b> (water/graphite) slows neutrons so they can be absorbed by nuclei.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
Basic	What is nuclear fusion and why is it difficult to achieve on Earth?	<b>Nuclear fusion:</b> two small nuclei (e.g. hydrogen isotopes — deuterium and tritium) <b>combine</b> under extreme conditions to form a larger nucleus, releasing enormous energy.<br><b>Why difficult:</b> nuclei are positively charged → repel each other → need <b>extremely high temperatures</b> (millions of °C) and high pressure to force nuclei close enough to fuse. Difficult to contain such a plasma. Current fusion reactors (e.g. JET) require more energy input than they produce.<br><b>Advantages if achieved:</b> huge energy release, abundant fuel (hydrogen from seawater), no long-lived radioactive waste, no CO₂. Artificial: {{c5::medical (X-rays/nuclear medicine)}}, nuclear power, nuclear weapons testing. Background radiation must be {{c6::subtracted}} from measurements in experiments. Basic	What is the difference between contamination and irradiation?	<b>Irradiation:</b> the body is exposed to radiation from a <b>source outside</b> the body. The source is not in contact — once removed, exposure stops. Less dangerous than contamination (depending on type).<br><b>Contamination:</b> radioactive material <b>gets onto or inside</b> the body (inhaled, ingested, skin contact). Continues to irradiate from inside — much harder to remove. Alpha emitters are especially dangerous as contaminants (very ionising internally) even though alpha is stopped by skin externally. Basic	Worked example: half-life calculation. A sample initially contains 6400 atoms of a radioactive isotope with half-life 3 years. How many atoms remain after 12 years?	Number of half-lives = 12 ÷ 3 = <b>4 half-lives</b><br>After 1 half-life (3 yr): 6400 ÷ 2 = 3200<br>After 2 half-lives (6 yr): 3200 ÷ 2 = 1600<br>After 3 half-lives (9 yr): 1600 ÷ 2 = 800<br>After 4 half-lives (12 yr): 800 ÷ 2 = <b>400 atoms</b><br><b>Formula shortcut:</b> N = N₀ × (½)ⁿ where n = number of half-lives. N = 6400 × (½)⁴ = 6400/16 = 400.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
Basic	How do you balance nuclear equations (alpha and beta decay)?	<b>Rules:</b> mass numbers (top) must balance. Atomic numbers (bottom) must balance.<br><b>Alpha decay:</b> α particle = ⁴₂He. Mass number −4, atomic number −2.<br>Example: ²³⁸₉₂U → ²³⁴₉₀Th + ⁴₂He ✓ (238=234+4; 92=90+2)<br><b>Beta decay:</b> β particle = ⁰₋₁e. Mass number unchanged, atomic number +1.<br>Example: ¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e ✓ (14=14+0; 6=7+(−1))<br><b>Key:</b> gamma (γ) emission — no change to mass or atomic number (just energy released). Basic	What are the dangers of ionising radiation and how do we protect against them?	<b>Dangers:</b> ionising radiation removes electrons from atoms → damages cells → kills cells (high doses) or causes mutations → <b>cancer</b>. Alpha most ionising (most dangerous if contaminating), gamma most penetrating (requires heavy shielding).<br><b>Reducing exposure — ALARA principle (As Low As Reasonably Achievable):</b><br>• <b>Time:</b> minimise time in radiation field<br>• <b>Distance:</b> stay as far away as possible<br>• <b>Shielding:</b> paper stops α; aluminium stops β; thick lead/concrete stops γ<br>• Wear dosimeters to monitor accumulated dose. Use tongs, not hands.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Atomic Structure
