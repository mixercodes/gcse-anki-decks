Basic	What is the difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells?	Eukaryotic cells (animal/plant) have a true membrane-bound nucleus. Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have no nucleus — DNA is a single circular strand in the cytoplasm. Prokaryotes have no mitochondria and are much smaller (1–10μm vs 10–100μm). Basic	What is mitosis used for?	Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells with the same number of chromosomes as the parent. Used for growth, repair and asexual reproduction. DNA replicates before division so both cells receive a complete copy.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Basic	What are stem cells and why are they medically important?	Undifferentiated cells that can divide and specialise into many cell types. Embryonic stem cells can become any cell type. Adult stem cells (e.g. bone marrow) are more limited. Used to treat leukaemia (bone marrow transplant) and potentially Parkinson's and diabetes. Therapeutic cloning prevents rejection.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Basic	What is the difference between diffusion, osmosis and active transport?	Diffusion: passive movement of particles from high to low concentration. Osmosis: passive movement of water molecules across a partially permeable membrane from high to low water potential. Active transport: movement of substances against a concentration gradient using energy (ATP) from respiration — e.g. uptake of mineral ions by root hair cells.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Cloze	Factors affecting rate of diffusion: {{c1::concentration gradient}} (steeper = faster), {{c2::surface area}} (larger = faster), {{c3::temperature}} (higher = faster), {{c4::distance}} (shorter = faster).		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Basic	What is the required practical for osmosis? What results would you expect?	<b>Method:</b> cut potato cylinders of equal length/mass. Place in different concentrations of sucrose solution (e.g. 0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0 mol/dm³). Leave 20–30 minutes. Remove, dry, re-weigh (or re-measure length).<br><b>Results:</b> in distilled water (hypotonic) → cylinder gains mass (water enters by osmosis). In concentrated solution (hypertonic) → cylinder loses mass (water leaves). At one concentration the mass doesn't change — this equals the concentration of cell sap (isotonic point).<br><b>Graph:</b> plot % change in mass vs concentration → straight line crossing x-axis.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Basic	What is the surface area to volume ratio (SA:V) and why is it important for organisms?	As an organism gets larger, its volume increases faster than its surface area — the SA:V ratio decreases.<br><b>Importance:</b> small organisms (bacteria, single cells) have large SA:V → enough diffusion surface to supply all cells. Large organisms need <b>specialised exchange surfaces</b> (lungs, villi, gills) to increase SA for gas/nutrient exchange.<br><b>Exam example:</b> a 1cm cube: SA = 6cm², V = 1cm³, SA:V = 6:1. A 2cm cube: SA = 24cm², V = 8cm³, SA:V = 3:1. As size doubles, SA:V halves.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Cell Biology
Basic	Describe the adaptations of specialised cells: red blood cell, sperm cell, nerve cell, and root hair cell.	<b>Red blood cell:</b> biconcave disc → large SA for O₂ absorption. No nucleus → more space for haemoglobin. Flexible → fits through narrow capillaries.<br><b>Sperm cell:</b> long tail (flagellum) for motility. Many mitochondria for energy. Acrosome contains enzymes to penetrate egg.<br><b>Nerve (neurone):</b> very long → transmits impulses far distances. Myelin sheath → insulates, speeds transmission.<br><b>Root hair cell:</b> long projection into soil → increased SA for water/mineral absorption. Large permanent vacuole → maintains osmotic gradient.
