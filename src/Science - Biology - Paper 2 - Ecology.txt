Cloze	Levels of ecological organisation: {{c1::individual}} → {{c2::population}} (same species in one area) → {{c3::community}} (all populations in one area) → {{c4::ecosystem}} (community + abiotic environment).		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What are abiotic and biotic factors?	Abiotic (non-living): temperature, light intensity, moisture, pH, wind speed, CO₂ levels, oxygen levels. Biotic (living): food availability, predators, competitors, disease. Both affect distribution and abundance of organisms.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What is a food chain and what do the terms mean?	Shows feeding relationships. Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer. Arrows show direction of energy transfer. Producers are photosynthetic plants. Decomposers (bacteria/fungi) break down dead matter, returning nutrients to soil.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Cloze	Biomass decreases at each trophic level because energy is lost through {{c1::respiration}}, {{c2::waste products}} (urine/faeces), and {{c3::heat}}. Typically only {{c4::10%}} of energy is transferred between levels.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What is the carbon cycle?	Carbon is recycled through: photosynthesis (CO₂ absorbed by plants). Respiration (CO₂ released by all organisms). Decomposition (CO₂ released by bacteria/fungi). Combustion (burning fossil fuels releases CO₂). Ocean absorption. Limestone/fossil fuels lock carbon away for millions of years.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What are the effects of human activities on ecosystems?	Deforestation (reduces CO₂ absorption, destroys habitats). Pollution (water: fertilisers/sewage causing eutrophication; air: acid rain, greenhouse gases). Peat bog destruction (releases stored CO₂). Overfishing. Intensive farming reduces biodiversity.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Cloze	Eutrophication: excess {{c1::nitrates/phosphates}} from fertilisers enter water → algae bloom → blocks light → plants die → decomposers use up {{c2::oxygen}} → aquatic life dies. Called {{c3::eutrophication}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What methods are used to protect biodiversity?	Breeding programmes for endangered species. Seed banks. Conservation of habitats (nature reserves, marine protected areas). Reforestation. Reducing pollution. Sustainable fishing (quotas, mesh size). International agreements (e.g. CITES on endangered species trade).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	What are quadrats and transects? How are they used to estimate population size?	<b>Quadrat:</b> a square frame placed randomly in a habitat. Count individuals of a species inside → calculate mean per m². Scale up to estimate total population: mean density × total area.<br><b>Random sampling:</b> use random number table to choose coordinates — avoids bias.<br><b>Transect:</b> a line across a habitat. Place quadrats at regular intervals along the line. Used to investigate how species distribution changes across a gradient (e.g. shore to inland, light to shade).<br><b>Mark-recapture method</b> (mobile animals): capture → mark → release → recapture. Population = (1st catch × 2nd catch) ÷ number of marked individuals in 2nd catch.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	Describe a predator-prey population graph and explain the relationship.	<b>Pattern:</b> both populations cycle. Prey population increases → predator population increases (more food). Predator population increases → prey population decreases (more predation). Prey population decreases → predator population decreases (food shortage). Predator decrease → prey population recovers → cycle repeats.<br><b>Key features of graph:</b> predator numbers lag behind prey numbers (takes time for predators to respond). Both oscillate — never reach zero in a stable ecosystem. Lines are out of phase (prey peaks before predator).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
Basic	How does global warming affect ecosystems?	<b>Rising temperatures:</b> species distribution shifts poleward (tracking their climate niche). Timing of seasonal events changes — earlier flowering/breeding may not match food availability → mismatches in food chains.<br><b>Melting ice:</b> loss of habitat for polar species (polar bears, Arctic foxes). Rising sea levels → loss of coastal habitats. Ocean acidification (CO₂ dissolved → carbonic acid) → damages coral reefs and shellfish (can't form shells).<br><b>Biodiversity loss:</b> species unable to adapt fast enough may become extinct. Invasive species spread into newly suitable areas, outcompeting native species.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Ecology
