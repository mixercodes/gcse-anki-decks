Basic	What are the four periods covered in People's Health and roughly what years do they cover?	Medieval (1250–1500), Early Modern (1500–1750), Industrial (1750–1900), Modern (1900–present). The theme is continuity and change in living conditions and government responses to disease across these periods.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What were medieval towns like for ordinary people? (housing, water, waste, food)	Housing: cramped, thatched, shared with animals (hovels). Smoke stayed inside; trades like tanning done indoors. Water: water sellers with leather sacks (went stagnant); conduits funded by monasteries; streams. Waste: cesspits emptied by gongfermers twice a year; rakers cleared streets; middens (waste heaps). Food: pottage (stew kept for days); rotten meat sold at markets; fresh fish/veg in the countryside.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Medieval water: in towns, water sellers used {{c1::leather sacks}} in which water quickly went {{c2::stagnant}}. Conduits (lead pipes) were often paid for by {{c3::monasteries}}. Most people collected water from {{c4::streams or rivers}}.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Medieval waste: {{c1::gongfermers}} emptied cesspits, usually only {{c2::twice a year}}. {{c3::Rakers}} swept the streets, but left them if too narrow. {{c4::Middens}} were waste heaps outside homes. Cesspits often {{c5::overflowed}} if not watertight.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	The Black Death arrived in England in {{c1::1348}} and eventually killed about {{c2::one third}} of Europe's population. There were three forms: {{c3::bubonic}} (swellings/buboes), {{c4::septicaemic}} (blood), and {{c5::pneumonic}} (lungs).		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What did medieval people believe caused the Black Death?	Punishment from God for sin. Jews had poisoned the water. Miasma (bad-smelling air). Volcano fumes. The movement of planets. The four humours being out of balance.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How did individuals and the Church respond to the Black Death?	Flagellants walked barefoot through streets whipping themselves to beg God's forgiveness. People tied live toads or chickens to their buboes. Others balanced the four humours by bloodletting. Rich people fled to the countryside to escape miasma. Sweet-smelling herbs were carried; candles were burned.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How did the government respond to the Black Death?	There was no national government — the king and Church acted locally. Edward I ordered York to clean its streets (1301). Edward III ordered London to clear up (1349). No centralised laws were passed. Local towns made their own rules. These were largely ineffective.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Medieval improvements: in 1329 the {{c1::butcher's guild of Winchester}} was set up to check meat quality. In 1415 the Mayor of London ordered {{c2::public latrines}} to be rebuilt. In 1385 a {{c3::warden}} was hired to check the Thames was clean. These were {{c4::local}} not national responses.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How did living conditions change (or not) in the Early Modern period (1500–1750)?	Some improvement: stone houses began replacing thatch for the wealthy; piped water arrived for the rich (1600s); flushing closet invented 1596. But for the poor: water sellers still common (now using barrels); cesspits still overflowed; main diet still pottage. Little changed for the majority.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	In the Early Modern period, {{c1::Henry VIII}} was the first monarch to issue national plague orders, requiring {{c2::40 days' isolation}} (1518). In 1578, {{c3::Elizabeth I}} introduced {{c4::17 Plague Orders}}, including burning victims' clothing, killing stray animals, and holding funerals after dusk. In 1604, James I added {{c5::hanging and whipping}} as punishments for ignoring plague orders.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What was the Gin Craze and why was it a public health problem?	In the early 1700s, cheap gin flooded London. By the 1720s: crime increased, families fell into poverty, death rates rose (alcohol-related), babies were harmed by mothers drinking while pregnant. Hundreds of thousands drank gin as an escape from hardship.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	The {{c1::1729, 1736 and 1743}} Gin Acts introduced taxes and licences for gin sellers but were {{c2::largely ineffective}} — the government did not enforce them and illegal gin shops flourished. The {{c3::1751}} Gin Act was much tougher: only people who paid rent and taxes could sell gin; illegal sellers faced {{c4::imprisonment and whipping}}, and gin drinking was hugely reduced.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What were the key features of Industrial-era living conditions (1750–1900)?	Housing: back-to-back houses; up to 8 people per room; lodging houses; cellars. Water: shared pumps serving up to 200 people; water drawn from polluted canals. Waste: shared privies over cesspools; 'night soil men' collected waste. Food: widespread adulteration (sawdust in bread, chalk in milk, copper in butter).	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Cholera first arrived in Britain in {{c1::1831–32}}, killing {{c2::32,000}} people. A second epidemic in {{c3::1877}} killed {{c4::12,000}}. Symptoms were {{c5::fever, diarrhoea and vomiting}}. Most believed it was spread by {{c6::miasma}} (bad air).		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	In 1842, {{c1::Edwin Chadwick}} published a report linking {{c2::poverty and dirt}} to disease. This led to the {{c3::first Public Health Act in 1848}}, which set up a Central Board of Health. Chadwick argued that {{c4::clean water and sewers}} would reduce disease and poverty.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	In 1854, {{c1::John Snow}} investigated a cholera outbreak in {{c2::Soho, London}}. He mapped deaths and found they centred around the {{c3::Broad Street water pump}}. He persuaded the council to {{c4::remove the pump handle}}, ending the outbreak. He proved cholera was spread through {{c5::contaminated water}}, not miasma.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	The {{c1::Great Stink}} of {{c2::1858}} made the smell from the River Thames so unbearable that Parliament could not sit. This forced the government to fund {{c3::Joseph Bazalgette}}'s sewer system — over {{c4::1,300 miles}} of sewers were built, cleaning up the Thames and reducing cholera.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	The {{c1::1875 Public Health Act}} (under Disraeli) made it {{c2::compulsory}} for local authorities to provide clean water, sewers, and housing inspections. Unlike the {{c3::1848}} Act, which was voluntary, the 1875 Act had real {{c4::enforcement powers}}.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	{{c1::Louis Pasteur}} developed germ theory in {{c2::1861}}, proving that {{c3::germs (microbes)}} — not miasma or bad air — caused disease. This was a turning point: governments could now target the {{c4::actual cause}} of disease rather than its symptoms.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	The Spanish Flu of {{c1::1918–19}} killed over {{c2::50 million}} people worldwide and over {{c3::228,000}} in the UK. Symptoms began as a common cold then became {{c4::pneumonia}}. The national government response was {{c5::limited}} — key action came from individuals like {{c6::Dr Niven}} in Manchester.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Dr {{c1::Niven}} in Manchester: published detailed advice in {{c2::newspapers and cinemas}}; kept {{c3::detailed case records}} to track patterns; urged councils to {{c4::shut schools and theatres}}; praised NHS workers. Only {{c5::2,000}} died in Manchester — far fewer than London or Glasgow.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What was the 'Homes for Heroes' initiative and who introduced it?	After WW1, Lloyd George's government promised to build council houses ('Homes for Heroes') for returning soldiers. Back-to-back housing was banned in 1909 and removed by 1919. However, Margaret Thatcher sold off council houses in the 1980s — they passed to private landlords who often failed to maintain hygiene standards.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Five phases of UK response to AIDS: 1. Growing {{c1::awareness}} (1970s–1983): first deaths reported, Terrence Higgins Trust set up. 2. Growing {{c2::alarm}} (1984–85): widespread fear and stigma. 3. Growing {{c3::understanding}} (1986–87): clean needle programmes, Princess Diana shook hands with AIDS patients. 4. Growing {{c4::acceptance}} (1988–95): Freddie Mercury concert raised £20 million for research. 5. Growing {{c5::complacency}} (1996+): anti-retroviral drugs led many to believe AIDS was curable.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	In {{c1::1987}}, {{c2::Princess Diana}} visited an AIDS clinic and shook hands with a patient, proving AIDS was {{c3::not spread by touch}}. This broke the social stigma and created widespread publicity. In {{c4::1992}}, a tribute concert for Freddie Mercury raised {{c5::£20 million}} for AIDS research.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	In {{c1::1996}}, scientists developed {{c2::anti-retroviral}} drugs that slowed the development of AIDS in HIV-positive patients. As a result, the government {{c3::relaxed its campaigns}}, leading to {{c4::growing complacency}}. By 2009, over {{c5::100,000}} people in Britain had HIV.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	What does RARE stand for in the context of Nazi Germany's impact on the German people? (Also used in People's Health for 20th century analysis)	Rationing, Area bombing, Refugees, Employment. A framework for analysing the impact of Total War (WW2) on Germany — or used by teachers to structure analysis of modern public health impacts.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How did monasteries contribute to medieval public health?	Infirmaries cared for the sick (Benedictine rule: 'care for the sick stands before all'). Herb/physick gardens grew medicinal plants. Monasteries built near running fresh water — provided drinking and washing water for the sick. Latrines built so waste discharged into rivers. Even without running water, cathedrals built pipes to supply fresh water. Monks believed caring for the sick brought them closer to God. Limited: only helped those who could reach a monastery; no national system.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	Leeds local board of health ({{c1::1831}}): set up in response to cholera. Employed surgeon {{c2::Dr Robert Baker}} to investigate. He {{c3::mapped}} the outbreak and found cases concentrated in {{c4::dirty areas}}. Baker believed in {{c5::miasma}} (incorrect), but his report was important evidence linking {{c6::dirt and disease}}. The board also published advice on {{c7::posters and newspapers}} and opened a {{c8::cholera hospital}}.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How can you classify the different responses to cholera in the 1830s?	Three belief categories: (1) Punishment from God — prayer, church attendance, fasting. (2) Miasma — cleaning streets, burning barrels of tar, adding chloride of lime to sewers to mask smells. (3) Contagion — quarantine (stopping people moving from town centres to suburbs), cholera hospitals to isolate victims. Most responses were based on miasma theory — incorrect, but accidentally useful (cleaning streets did reduce disease). John Snow's 1854 waterborne proof was not widely accepted for years.	History::Paper 1::People's Health
Cloze	{{c1::Laissez-faire}}: the political belief that the government should {{c2::not interfere}} in people's everyday lives. This was the main reason the 1848 Public Health Act was {{c3::voluntary}} — the government did not feel it had the right to force local councils to act. It also explains why the {{c4::1751 Gin Act}} was tougher than earlier acts — eventually public opinion shifted enough to force action. Laissez-faire slowed health reform throughout the {{c5::Industrial period}}.		History::Paper 1::People's Health
Basic	How do you structure the People's Health 18-mark essay (individuals vs government)?	Typical question: 'The actions of great individuals were the most important factor in improving Public Health.' Structure: Para 1 (agree — individual, e.g. John Snow). Para 2 (agree — individual, e.g. Bazalgette/Chadwick). Para 3 (disagree — government, e.g. 1875 Public Health Act compulsory). Para 4 (disagree — events forcing change, e.g. Great Stink, cholera epidemics). Conclusion: both individuals AND government were needed — individuals provided the evidence; government provided the enforcement. Link to laissez-faire as the brake on progress. Always name which concept you are using — e.g. 'this shows continuity because...' or 'a consequence of this was...'. The 9-mark summary question requires at least two different second-order concepts.
