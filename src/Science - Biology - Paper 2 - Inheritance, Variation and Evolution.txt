Basic	What is the difference between meiosis and mitosis?	Mitosis: produces 2 identical diploid cells — for growth/repair. Meiosis: produces 4 non-identical haploid gametes (sex cells) — for sexual reproduction. Meiosis produces genetic variation (cells are not identical to each other or to the parent cell). Basic	What is the difference between dominant, recessive, homozygous and heterozygous?	Dominant allele: always expressed if present (shown with capital letter). Recessive: only expressed if two copies are present (lowercase). Homozygous: two identical alleles (AA or aa). Heterozygous: two different alleles (Aa) — the dominant allele is expressed (phenotype), but the recessive is carried (genotype).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Cloze	Cystic fibrosis: caused by a {{c1::recessive}} allele. Both parents must be {{c2::carriers}} (Ff) for a 1 in 4 chance of an affected child (ff). Polydactyly: caused by a {{c3::dominant}} allele — only ONE copy needed.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Basic	What is natural selection and how does it lead to evolution?	Individuals vary due to random mutations. Those with beneficial variations are better adapted to their environment (survival of the fittest). They survive to reproduce, passing on the advantageous alleles. Over many generations the population changes — evolution. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria is a key example.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution

Basic	How are fossils formed?	Fossils are the remains of organisms from millions of years ago preserved in rock. They form in three main ways: 1) Hard parts (bones, shells, teeth) that do not decay are slowly replaced by minerals. 2) Traces left behind — footprints, burrows, or rootlets preserved in rock. 3) Whole organisms preserved where decay is prevented — in ice, amber (tree resin), or very dry/acidic conditions.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Basic	Why is the fossil record incomplete? What does it still tell us?	The fossil record is incomplete because: most organisms have soft bodies that decay before fossilisation can occur; many fossils that did form have not yet been found or have been destroyed by geological activity. Despite this, the fossil record provides evidence that life on Earth has changed over time (evolution) — fossils show simple organisms in older rock layers and more complex organisms in more recent layers. Basic	What is extinction and what causes it?	Extinction: a species no longer exists. Caused by: new predators/competitors/pathogens, habitat destruction, climate change, catastrophic events. Mass extinctions (e.g. dinosaurs, asteroid impact). Genetic diversity is reduced by selective breeding and extinction — making species vulnerable. Basic	What is genetic engineering and what are its uses?	Inserting a gene from one organism into the genome of another using restriction enzymes and ligase. Uses: GM bacteria produce human insulin for diabetics. Golden rice modified to contain beta-carotene (Vitamin A). GM crops with herbicide/pest resistance. Concerns: biodiversity, unknown risks, ethics.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution

Basic	What is sex-linked inheritance? Use red-green colour blindness as an example.	The gene for colour blindness is on the <b>X chromosome</b> (sex-linked). Recessive allele (Xᵇ) = colour blind. Dominant allele (Xᴮ) = normal vision.<br><b>Possible genotypes:</b><br>Females: XᴮXᴮ (normal), XᴮXᵇ (carrier — normal vision but can pass on), XᵇXᵇ (colour blind — rare)<br>Males: XᴮY (normal), XᵇY (colour blind — only need ONE recessive allele as Y has no corresponding gene)<br><b>Why more common in males:</b> males have only one X — if they inherit Xᵇ, they are colour blind. Basic	What is selective breeding (artificial selection) and what are the risks?	<b>Process:</b> choose individuals with desired characteristics → breed them → select offspring with best characteristics → repeat over many generations.<br><b>Examples:</b> cattle bred for high milk yield, dogs bred for temperament, crop plants for disease resistance/yield/taste.<br><b>Risks:</b> reduces genetic diversity (gene pool becomes narrow) → population vulnerable to new diseases. Inbreeding → harmful recessive alleles become more common (e.g. hip dysplasia in dogs, reduced disease resistance in crops). If a new disease emerges → entire population may be wiped out. Basic	Who were Darwin and Wallace and what did they contribute?	Charles Darwin published 'On the Origin of Species' (1859) proposing natural selection as the mechanism for evolution. Alfred Russel Wallace independently developed the same theory and corresponded with Darwin — both presented their ideas simultaneously. Key idea: individuals vary → those with advantageous traits survive and reproduce → advantageous alleles passed on → species change over generations. Initially controversial because it challenged religion and because Darwin couldn't explain how traits were inherited (Mendel's genetics was not yet known). Eventually accepted as fossil evidence, DNA analysis and observable evolution (e.g. antibiotic resistance) accumulated.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Cloze	Variation in a species can be caused by {{c1::genetic}} factors (different alleles from mutations and sexual reproduction), {{c2::environmental}} factors (diet, climate, disease), or {{c3::a combination of both}}. Example: height is influenced by {{c4::genes}} (determining maximum potential) and {{c5::nutrition}} (determining whether that potential is reached). Continuous variation (e.g. height) shows a {{c6::normal distribution}}; discontinuous variation (e.g. blood group) falls into {{c7::distinct categories}}. Cloze	A {{c1::mutation}} is a random change to the {{c2::sequence of bases in DNA}}. Most mutations are {{c3::neutral}} (no effect on protein/phenotype). Some are {{c4::harmful}} (e.g. cancer-causing). Rarely, a mutation may be {{c5::beneficial}} — giving a survival advantage, and being selected for via {{c6::natural selection}}. Mutations are the source of all {{c7::new alleles}} and are the basis of all genetic variation.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Basic	What is speciation and how does it occur?	Speciation is the formation of a new species. Occurs when a population is split by a geographical barrier (e.g. mountain, sea). The two groups are isolated — no interbreeding. Each group faces different selection pressures → different mutations become advantageous → allele frequencies change differently in each population → over many generations, the two populations become so genetically different they can no longer interbreed successfully — two distinct species exist. Example: Darwin's finches on different Galapagos islands evolved different beak shapes to suit different food sources.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Cloze	Cloning methods: {{c1::tissue culture}} — small group of cells from a plant grown on nutrient agar into identical plants. {{c2::Embryo transplantation}} — split embryo into multiple identical embryos → implant into surrogate mothers. {{c3::Adult cell cloning (somatic cell nuclear transfer)}} — remove nucleus from egg cell, insert nucleus from adult cell, stimulate to divide, implant into surrogate (produced Dolly the sheep).
