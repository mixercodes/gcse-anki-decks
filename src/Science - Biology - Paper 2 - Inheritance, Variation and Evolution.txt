Basic	What is the difference between meiosis and mitosis?	Mitosis: produces 2 identical diploid cells — for growth/repair. Meiosis: produces 4 non-identical haploid gametes (sex cells) — for sexual reproduction. Meiosis produces genetic variation through crossing over and random assortment of chromosomes. Basic	What is the difference between dominant, recessive, homozygous and heterozygous?	Dominant allele: always expressed if present (shown with capital letter). Recessive: only expressed if two copies are present (lowercase). Homozygous: two identical alleles (AA or aa). Heterozygous: two different alleles (Aa) — the dominant allele is expressed (phenotype), but the recessive is carried (genotype).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Cloze	Cystic fibrosis: caused by a {{c1::recessive}} allele. Both parents must be {{c2::carriers}} (Ff) for a 1 in 4 chance of an affected child (ff). Polydactyly: caused by a {{c3::dominant}} allele — only ONE copy needed.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Basic	What is natural selection and how does it lead to evolution?	Individuals vary due to random mutations. Those with beneficial variations are better adapted to their environment (survival of the fittest). They survive to reproduce, passing on the advantageous alleles. Over many generations the population changes — evolution. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria is a key example.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Cloze	Evidence for evolution: {{c1::fossil record}} (gradual changes over time), {{c2::antibiotic resistance}} (observable evolution), {{c3::DNA analysis}} (similar sequences in related species), {{c4::comparative anatomy}} (homologous structures in different species). Basic	What is extinction and what causes it?	Extinction: a species no longer exists. Caused by: new predators/competitors/pathogens, habitat destruction, climate change, catastrophic events. Mass extinctions (e.g. dinosaurs, asteroid impact). Genetic diversity is reduced by selective breeding and extinction — making species vulnerable. Basic	What is genetic engineering and what are its uses?	Inserting a gene from one organism into the genome of another using restriction enzymes and ligase. Uses: GM bacteria produce human insulin for diabetics. Golden rice modified to contain beta-carotene (Vitamin A). GM crops with herbicide/pest resistance. Concerns: biodiversity, unknown risks, ethics.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution

Basic	What is sex-linked inheritance? Use red-green colour blindness as an example.	The gene for colour blindness is on the <b>X chromosome</b> (sex-linked). Recessive allele (Xᵇ) = colour blind. Dominant allele (Xᴮ) = normal vision.<br><b>Possible genotypes:</b><br>Females: XᴮXᴮ (normal), XᴮXᵇ (carrier — normal vision but can pass on), XᵇXᵇ (colour blind — rare)<br>Males: XᴮY (normal), XᵇY (colour blind — only need ONE recessive allele as Y has no corresponding gene)<br><b>Why more common in males:</b> males have only one X — if they inherit Xᵇ, they are colour blind. Females need TWO copies of Xᵇ (one from each parent).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Inheritance, Variation and Evolution
Basic	What is selective breeding (artificial selection) and what are the risks?	<b>Process:</b> choose individuals with desired characteristics → breed them → select offspring with best characteristics → repeat over many generations.<br><b>Examples:</b> cattle bred for high milk yield, dogs bred for temperament, crop plants for disease resistance/yield/taste.<br><b>Risks:</b> reduces genetic diversity (gene pool becomes narrow) → population vulnerable to new diseases. Inbreeding → harmful recessive alleles become more common (e.g. hip dysplasia in dogs, reduced disease resistance in crops). If a new disease emerges → entire population may be wiped out.
