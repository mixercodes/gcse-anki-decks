Basic	What is homeostasis and why is it important?	Homeostasis is the regulation of the internal conditions of a cell or organism to maintain optimum conditions for enzyme action and cell function. Examples: blood glucose concentration, body temperature (37°C), water levels.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response

Basic	What is a reflex arc and why are reflexes important?	A reflex arc: receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (in spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector. Reflexes are automatic and fast because they bypass the conscious brain — protects the body from harm (e.g. pulling hand away from heat).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	How does the body control blood glucose levels?	Monitored by the pancreas. If too high: pancreas releases insulin → glucose taken up by cells and liver (converted to glycogen) → blood glucose falls. Type 1 diabetes: pancreas produces no insulin. Type 2: cells stop responding to insulin. <b>(HT only:</b> if too low, glucagon released → glycogen converted back to glucose → blood glucose rises.<b>)</b>	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Cloze	Type 1 diabetes is treated by {{c1::insulin injections}} and monitoring diet. Type 2 diabetes is managed by {{c2::diet and exercise}} — losing weight can reverse it. Type 2 is linked to {{c3::obesity}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	How does the body regulate temperature?	Thermoregulation centre in the hypothalamus detects blood temperature changes. Too hot: vasodilation (more blood to skin surface), sweating (evaporation cools). Too cold: vasoconstriction (less blood to skin), shivering (muscle contractions generate heat), hair erection (trap air layer).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	What is the role of hormones in the menstrual cycle?	FSH (from pituitary): stimulates egg maturation and oestrogen production. Oestrogen: thickens uterus lining. LH (from pituitary): triggers ovulation (~day 14). Progesterone: maintains uterus lining after ovulation; falls if no fertilisation → menstruation. <b>(HT only:</b> oestrogen inhibits FSH and stimulates LH release.<b>)</b>	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response

Basic	What are the methods of contraception and how do they work?	<b>Hormonal methods</b> (all prevent egg maturation by inhibiting FSH/LH):<br>• Combined pill (oestrogen + progesterone) — very effective, taken daily<br>• Progesterone-only pill (mini pill) — thickens cervical mucus, inhibits ovulation<br>• Injection/implant/patch/hormonal IUD (Mirena) — slow release of progesterone, long-acting<br><b>Non-hormonal methods:</b><br>• Condom (male/female) — barrier method, also protects against STIs<br>• Diaphragm — barrier, used with spermicide<br>• Copper IUD — toxic to sperm, also prevents implantation; non-hormonal, long-lasting<br>• Abstinence — no sexual intercourse<br>• Sterilisation (vasectomy/tubal ligation) — surgical, permanent<br><b>Key exam point:</b> hormonal methods prevent egg maturation; barrier methods prevent sperm reaching egg; IUD prevents implantation or fertilisation. Basic	How do the kidneys control water balance (osmoregulation)? What is the role of ADH?	<b>Kidneys</b> filter blood under high pressure → produce urine. Useful substances (glucose, water, some ions) are selectively reabsorbed back into the blood.<br><b>ADH (antidiuretic hormone)</b> — released by pituitary gland:<br>• If blood water potential is TOO LOW (dehydrated): ADH released → kidneys reabsorb MORE water → concentrated, small volume urine.<br>• If blood water potential is TOO HIGH (overhydrated): less ADH → kidneys reabsorb LESS water → dilute, large volume urine.<br><b>Negative feedback loop:</b> hypothalamus detects → pituitary releases/stops ADH → kidney responds → blood water potential changes → hypothalamus detects again. Basic	[HT only] How does thyroxine illustrate the principle of negative feedback?	<b>Thyroxine</b> is produced by the thyroid gland and regulates metabolic rate.<br><b>Negative feedback:</b><br>1. Hypothalamus detects low thyroxine → releases TRH<br>2. Pituitary releases TSH (thyroid stimulating hormone)<br>3. Thyroid gland produces more thyroxine<br>4. High thyroxine level detected by hypothalamus → TRH stops → TSH stops → thyroxine production falls.<br><b>Key principle of negative feedback:</b> the output (thyroxine) feeds back to inhibit the control system → maintains a stable concentration. All hormones in homeostasis use this mechanism.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	[HT only] What is adrenaline and what are its effects?	Adrenaline is a hormone produced by the adrenal glands (above the kidneys) in response to stress, danger, or excitement. It prepares the body for 'fight or flight': increases heart rate (more O₂ to muscles), increases blood glucose (glycogen converted to glucose), dilates pupils, diverts blood from digestive system to muscles. Unlike most hormones, adrenaline acts very quickly — released directly into the blood and triggers rapid physiological changes.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	What are the plant hormones and their commercial uses?	<b>Auxin (IAA):</b> produced at shoot tip. Causes cell elongation. More in shaded side → shoot bends towards light (phototropism). Commercial uses: rooting powder (cuttings grow roots), selective weedkillers (causes broad-leaf weeds to grow uncontrollably while grass is unaffected).<br><b>Gibberellins:</b> promote stem elongation and seed germination. Commercial use: seedless grapes, malting barley (beer production).<br><b>Ethene:</b> promotes fruit ripening. Commercial use: ripen fruit during transport/storage (expose to ethene gas).
