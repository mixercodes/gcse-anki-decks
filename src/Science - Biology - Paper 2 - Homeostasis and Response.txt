Basic	What is homeostasis and why is it important?	Homeostasis is the regulation of the internal conditions of a cell or organism to maintain optimum conditions for enzyme action and cell function. Examples: blood glucose concentration, body temperature (37°C), water levels.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Cloze	The nervous system: stimulus → {{c1::receptor}} → {{c2::sensory neurone}} → {{c3::CNS (brain/spinal cord)}} → {{c4::motor neurone}} → {{c5::effector}} (muscle or gland) → response.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Cloze	A synapse is a junction between neurones. The nerve impulse triggers release of {{c1::neurotransmitters}} which diffuse across the {{c2::synaptic gap}} and bind to receptors on the {{c3::next neurone}}, creating a new impulse.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	What is a reflex arc and why are reflexes important?	A reflex arc: receptor → sensory neurone → relay neurone (in spinal cord) → motor neurone → effector. Reflexes are automatic and fast because they bypass the conscious brain — protects the body from harm (e.g. pulling hand away from heat).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	How does the body control blood glucose levels?	Monitored by the pancreas. If too high: pancreas releases insulin → glucose taken up by cells and liver (converted to glycogen) → blood glucose falls. If too low: pancreas releases glucagon → glycogen converted back to glucose → blood glucose rises. Type 1 diabetes: pancreas produces no insulin. Type 2: cells stop responding to insulin.	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Cloze	Type 1 diabetes is treated by {{c1::insulin injections}} and monitoring diet. Type 2 diabetes is managed by {{c2::diet and exercise}} — losing weight can reverse it. Type 2 is linked to {{c3::obesity}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	How does the body regulate temperature?	Thermoregulation centre in the hypothalamus detects blood temperature changes. Too hot: vasodilation (more blood to skin surface), sweating (evaporation cools). Too cold: vasoconstriction (less blood to skin), shivering (muscle contractions generate heat), hair erection (trap air layer).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Basic	What is the role of hormones in the menstrual cycle?	FSH (from pituitary): stimulates egg maturation and oestrogen production. Oestrogen: thickens uterus lining, inhibits FSH, stimulates LH. LH (from pituitary): triggers ovulation (day 14). Progesterone: maintains uterus lining. Falls if no fertilisation → lining shed (menstruation).	Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
Cloze	Contraceptive pills contain {{c1::oestrogen and/or progesterone}} which {{c2::inhibit FSH production}}, preventing egg maturation. IVF uses {{c3::FSH}} to stimulate multiple egg production.		Science::Biology::Paper 2::Homeostasis and Response
