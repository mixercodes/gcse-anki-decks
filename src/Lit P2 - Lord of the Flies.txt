Basic	How does Lord of the Flies respond to The Coral Island?	The Coral Island (1858, R.M. Ballantyne) shows three boys marooned on an island who remain civilised and heroic. Golding was 'unsatisfied with how it espoused myths' and deliberately inverts it — in LOTF the boys are not saved by their inherent goodness but destroyed by their inherent evil. Golding believed man was 'diseased and fallen', not perfectible.	English Lit::Paper 2::Lord of the Flies

War allegory: Piggy = the Jews (victimised, vulnerable), Jack = Nazi leader (manipulates, brutalises). Philosophical: Freud (id/ego/superego), Darwin (regression), Maslow (hierarchy of needs). Basic	How is Simon presented in Lord of the Flies?	Simon is a Christ-figure — he retreats alone into nature, has a visionary encounter with evil (the LOTF), and is killed before he can share the truth. He alone understands 'the beast' is within the boys themselves. His death in Chapter 9 is the moral turning point of the novel — the boys have killed true goodness. His body is carried out to sea 'with a brightness' around it — a halo image.	English Lit::Paper 2::Lord of the Flies
Basic	How does fire develop symbolically?	Fire has dual symbolism. Controlled = hope, civilisation, rescue (Ralph's signal fire). Destructive = savagery (Jack lets fire die to hunt; boys' fire on Castle Rock destroys). The final irony: Jack sets the whole island ablaze to hunt Ralph, but the smoke brings the naval officer. Destruction accidentally achieves rescue. Golding suggests civilisation's collapse may paradoxically be what saves us.	English Lit::Paper 2::Lord of the Flies
