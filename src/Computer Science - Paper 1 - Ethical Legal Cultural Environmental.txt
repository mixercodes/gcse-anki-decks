Basic	What does the Data Protection Act 2018 require?	The DPA 2018 implements GDPR in UK law. Personal data must be:<br>• Collected for a <b>specified, explicit purpose</b> and not used beyond that<br>• <b>Adequate, relevant</b> and limited to what is necessary<br>• <b>Accurate</b> and kept up to date<br>• <b>Not kept longer than necessary</b><br>• Processed <b>securely</b><br>• Not transferred outside the UK without adequate protection<br><b>Individual rights:</b> right to access, correct, delete their data; right to object to processing. Basic	What three offences does the Computer Misuse Act 1990 define?	<b>Section 1 — Unauthorised access to computer material:</b> hacking into someone's account/system without permission. Up to 2 years imprisonment.<br><b>Section 2 — Unauthorised access with intent to commit further offences:</b> hacking to commit fraud, blackmail, etc. Up to 5 years.<br><b>Section 3 — Unauthorised modification of computer data:</b> installing malware, deleting others' files, altering data. Up to 10 years.	Computer Science::Paper 1::Ethical Legal Cultural Environmental
Basic	What does the Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 protect?	It protects the <b>intellectual property</b> of creators. Makes it illegal to:<br>• Copy, distribute or sell someone's software, music, films, images or written content without permission<br>• Modify copyrighted software<br>• Software <b>piracy</b> (illegal copying/distribution)<br>Covers both paid and free software. Copyright is automatic — no registration needed.<br><b>Software licences</b> define what users can legally do with purchased software.	Computer Science::Paper 1::Ethical Legal Cultural Environmental
Basic	What is the difference between open source and proprietary software?	<b>Open source:</b> source code is publicly available; free to use, modify, and distribute; community-developed and supported; examples: Linux, Firefox, LibreOffice, Python. Advantages: free, customisable, transparent (auditable for security).<br><b>Proprietary (closed source):</b> source code is kept secret; requires purchasing a licence; commercially developed; examples: Windows, Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop. Advantages: dedicated support, polished UX, accountability.	Computer Science::Paper 1::Ethical Legal Cultural Environmental
Basic	What is the digital divide?	The digital divide is the inequality between those who have access to digital technology (internet, computers, smartphones) and those who do not.<br><b>Causes:</b> cost of devices and internet access, lack of digital skills (digital literacy), geographical isolation (rural/developing areas), physical disability, age.<br><b>Consequences:</b> reduced access to education, employment opportunities, healthcare information, government services, and social connection — widening broader social inequality. Basic	What are the environmental impacts of digital technology?	<b>Negative impacts:</b><br>• Manufacturing devices uses rare earth minerals (mining causes habitat destruction) and creates carbon emissions<br>• Data centres consume vast amounts of electricity and water for cooling<br>• E-waste from discarded devices contains toxic materials (lead, mercury) — often exported to developing countries<br>• Planned obsolescence encourages frequent device replacement<br><b>Positive impacts:</b><br>• Paperless communication reduces deforestation<br>• Remote working reduces commuting emissions<br>• Smart technology can improve energy efficiency	Computer Science::Paper 1::Ethical Legal Cultural Environmental
Basic	What is a digital footprint and what are the privacy implications?	A digital footprint is the trail of data created by online activity — websites visited, social media posts, purchases, search history, emails, cookies, app usage, location data.<br><b>Passive footprint:</b> created without actively submitting information (cookies tracking browsing).<br><b>Active footprint:</b> created by deliberate actions (posting, registering, purchasing).<br><b>Implications:</b> data used for targeted advertising, profiling by employers/universities, can be hacked or leaked, difficult to permanently erase, governments may monitor it. Basic	What ethical issues surround artificial intelligence?	<b>Algorithmic bias:</b> AI trained on biased data produces biased outputs (e.g. facial recognition less accurate for darker skin tones; biased hiring algorithms).<br><b>Privacy:</b> AI systems process vast amounts of personal data.<br><b>Job displacement:</b> automation replacing workers in manufacturing, transport, customer service.<br><b>Autonomous weapons:</b> AI-controlled weapons with no human moral judgement.<br><b>Deepfakes:</b> AI-generated fake videos/audio used for disinformation.<br><b>Accountability:</b> unclear who is responsible when AI causes harm. Basic	What are the cultural impacts of digital technology?	<b>Positive:</b> global communication and collaboration; preservation of endangered languages and cultures online; increased access to education (MOOCs, YouTube); breaking down geographical barriers.<br><b>Negative:</b> cultural homogenisation (dominant cultures overpower minority ones online); addiction and reduced face-to-face interaction; misinformation spreading rapidly; cyberbullying; privacy erosion; surveillance capitalism. Basic	What is meant by ethical vs legal issues in computing?	<b>Legal issues</b> concern what is actually permitted or prohibited by law (Computer Misuse Act, DPA, Copyright Act). Breaking the law carries legal penalties.<br><b>Ethical issues</b> concern what society considers right or wrong — even if something is legal, it may be considered unethical. Example: a company legally collecting and selling users' data may be considered ethically wrong even though it is permitted.	Computer Science::Paper 1::Ethical Legal Cultural Environmental
