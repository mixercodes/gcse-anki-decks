Basic	What is the reactivity series and why is it useful?	Potassium, Sodium, Lithium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Carbon, Zinc, Iron, Tin, Lead, Hydrogen, Copper, Silver, Gold, Platinum (most to least reactive). More reactive metals displace less reactive ones from solutions. Carbon can reduce metal oxides above it in the series.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	Displacement reaction: {{c1::zinc}} + copper sulfate → {{c2::zinc sulfate}} + {{c3::copper}}. Zinc is more reactive so it {{c4::displaces}} copper. This is also a {{c5::redox}} reaction — zinc is {{c6::oxidised}} (loses electrons), copper is {{c7::reduced}} (gains electrons).		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	How is aluminium extracted and why is this method used?	Aluminium is extracted by electrolysis of molten aluminium oxide (bauxite) because it is too reactive to be reduced by carbon. Very energy-intensive and expensive. The cathode (negative) attracts Al³⁺ ions which gain electrons (reduction). The anode (positive) attracts O²⁻ ions which lose electrons (oxidation) — oxygen released.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	How is iron extracted in the blast furnace?	Iron ore (haematite, Fe₂O₃) + coke (carbon) + limestone in a blast furnace. Hot air blasted in — coke burns to form CO₂, then CO. CO reduces iron oxide: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. Limestone (CaCO₃) removes acidic impurities as slag. Molten iron collected at bottom.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What is electrolysis and what happens at each electrode?	Uses electricity to break down ionic compounds. Only works when ions are free to move (molten or dissolved). Cathode (negative): positive ions gain electrons — reduction. Anode (positive): negative ions lose electrons — oxidation. OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain. Cloze	Electrolysis of brine (NaCl solution): cathode produces {{c1::hydrogen}} gas. Anode produces {{c2::chlorine}} gas. Remaining solution becomes {{c3::sodium hydroxide}}. Products used for: bleach ({{c4::Cl₂ + NaOH}}), plastics ({{c5::Cl₂}}), cleaning agents ({{c6::NaOH}}).		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What happens during electrolysis of a molten ionic compound?	When an ionic compound is molten, ions are free to move. Only two ions present — both discharge. Example: molten lead bromide (PbBr₂). Cathode (−): Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb (lead metal deposited). Anode (+): 2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻ (bromine gas released). Molten compounds are simpler than aqueous — no water, no competition between ions. Another example: molten aluminium oxide in Hall-Héroult process — Al³⁺ discharged at cathode (aluminium metal), O²⁻ discharged at anode (oxygen gas).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	Electrolysis of molten lead bromide: at the cathode, {{c1::Pb²⁺}} ions gain {{c2::2 electrons}} → {{c3::lead metal}} deposited. At the anode, {{c4::2Br⁻}} ions lose {{c5::2 electrons}} → {{c6::bromine gas}} released. Molten compounds only contain {{c7::two types of ion}} — no water means no {{c8::competition}}. Basic	What are the rules for predicting products of electrolysis of aqueous solutions?	In aqueous solution, water provides competing H⁺ and OH⁻ ions. CATHODE (reduction): if the metal is BELOW hydrogen in the reactivity series → metal deposited. If the metal is ABOVE hydrogen → hydrogen gas produced instead (H⁺ discharged). ANODE (oxidation): if halide ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present in HIGH concentration → halogen gas produced. Otherwise → oxygen gas produced from OH⁻ ions (from water). Examples: CuSO₄(aq) — cathode: Cu (copper below H); anode: O₂ (no halide). NaCl(aq) — cathode: H₂ (sodium above H); anode: Cl₂ (high Cl⁻ concentration).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	Electrolysis of aqueous solutions — cathode rule: if the metal is {{c1::below hydrogen}} in the reactivity series, {{c2::metal}} is deposited. If the metal is {{c3::above hydrogen}}, {{c4::hydrogen gas}} is produced. Anode rule: if {{c5::halide ions}} (Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻) are present in high concentration, {{c6::halogen gas}} is produced. Otherwise {{c7::oxygen}} is produced from {{c8::OH⁻}} ions in water.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What happens when acids react with metals, bases and carbonates?	Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen. Acid + base → salt + water (neutralisation). Acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂. The name of the salt depends on the acid: HCl → chloride, H₂SO₄ → sulfate, HNO₃ → nitrate.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes

Weak acids (e.g. ethanoic acid) only {{c7::partially ionise}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	[HT only] What are half equations and how do you write them for electrolysis?	Half equations show what happens at each electrode during electrolysis.<br><b>At cathode (reduction — gain of electrons):</b><br>Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (copper deposited)<br>2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ (hydrogen gas)<br><b>At anode (oxidation — loss of electrons):</b><br>2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (chlorine gas)<br>4OH⁻ → 2H₂O + O₂ + 4e⁻ (oxygen gas from water)<br><b>Key rule:</b> electrons appear on the right of anode equations and on the left of cathode equations. Balance charges, then atoms.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What happens during the electrolysis of copper sulfate solution with copper electrodes? Why is this used in electroplating?	With copper electrodes: copper dissolves from the anode (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻) and is deposited at the cathode (Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu). The concentration of CuSO₄ stays constant — pure copper transferred from anode to cathode.<br><b>Purification of copper:</b> impure copper anode dissolves, pure copper deposited on cathode.<br><b>Electroplating:</b> object to plate = cathode. Plating metal = anode. Electrolyte contains ions of the plating metal. Used to: protect against corrosion (chromium-plated car parts), improve appearance (gold-plated jewellery), reduce costs (use cheap metal coated with silver). Basic	[HT only] How do you write ionic equations and what are spectator ions?	<b>Ionic equations</b> show only the ions/atoms that actually react — spectator ions are not included.<br><b>Spectator ions:</b> ions present in solution that don't change during the reaction.<br><b>Example:</b> HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l)<br>Full ionic equation: H⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → Na⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq) + H₂O(l)<br>Spectator ions (Na⁺, Cl⁻) cancel out → <b>net ionic equation: H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)</b><br>This is the ionic equation for ANY neutralisation of strong acid and base.
