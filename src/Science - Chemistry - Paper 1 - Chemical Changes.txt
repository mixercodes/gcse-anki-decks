Basic	What is the reactivity series and why is it useful?	Potassium, Sodium, Lithium, Calcium, Magnesium, Aluminium, Carbon, Zinc, Iron, Tin, Lead, Hydrogen, Copper, Silver, Gold, Platinum (most to least reactive). More reactive metals displace less reactive ones from solutions. Carbon can reduce metal oxides above it in the series.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	Displacement reaction: {{c1::zinc}} + copper sulfate → {{c2::zinc sulfate}} + {{c3::copper}}. Zinc is more reactive so it {{c4::displaces}} copper. This is also a {{c5::redox}} reaction — zinc is {{c6::oxidised}} (loses electrons), copper is {{c7::reduced}} (gains electrons).		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	How is aluminium extracted and why is this method used?	Aluminium is extracted by electrolysis of molten aluminium oxide (bauxite) because it is too reactive to be reduced by carbon. Very energy-intensive and expensive. The cathode (negative) attracts Al³⁺ ions which gain electrons (reduction). The anode (positive) attracts O²⁻ ions which lose electrons (oxidation) — oxygen released.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	How is iron extracted in the blast furnace?	Iron ore (haematite, Fe₂O₃) + coke (carbon) + limestone in a blast furnace. Hot air blasted in — coke burns to form CO₂, then CO. CO reduces iron oxide: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂. Limestone (CaCO₃) removes acidic impurities as slag. Molten iron collected at bottom.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What is electrolysis and what happens at each electrode?	Uses electricity to break down ionic compounds. Only works when ions are free to move (molten or dissolved). Cathode (negative): positive ions gain electrons — reduction. Anode (positive): negative ions lose electrons — oxidation. OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	Electrolysis of brine (NaCl solution): cathode produces {{c1::hydrogen}} gas. Anode produces {{c2::chlorine}} gas. Remaining solution becomes {{c3::sodium hydroxide}}. Products used for: bleach ({{c4::Cl₂ + NaOH}}), plastics ({{c5::Cl₂}}), cleaning agents ({{c6::NaOH}}).		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Basic	What happens when acids react with metals, bases and carbonates?	Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen. Acid + base → salt + water (neutralisation). Acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂. The name of the salt depends on the acid: HCl → chloride, H₂SO₄ → sulfate, HNO₃ → nitrate.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
Cloze	The pH scale runs from {{c1::0}} to {{c2::14}}. pH {{c3::below 7}} = acidic, pH {{c4::7}} = neutral, pH {{c5::above 7}} = alkaline. Strong acids (e.g. HCl) fully {{c6::ionise}} in water. Weak acids (e.g. ethanoic acid) only {{c7::partially ionise}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Chemical Changes
