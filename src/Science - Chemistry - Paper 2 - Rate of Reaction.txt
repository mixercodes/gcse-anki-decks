Basic	What four factors affect the rate of reaction and why?	Concentration (more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions). Temperature (particles move faster → more frequent AND more energetic collisions → more exceed activation energy). Surface area (more area exposed → more collisions). Catalyst (lowers activation energy → more particles have enough energy to react).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction
Basic	What is a catalyst and how does it work?	A substance that increases the rate of a reaction without being used up. Provides an alternative reaction pathway with a lower activation energy. Biological catalysts are called enzymes. Heterogeneous catalysts (different state to reactants) — e.g. iron in Haber process.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction

Basic	What is a reversible reaction and what is dynamic equilibrium?	A reversible reaction can proceed in both directions (⇌). Dynamic equilibrium: in a closed system, the forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate — concentrations remain constant. <b>(HT only:</b> Le Chatelier's principle — if conditions change, the equilibrium shifts to oppose the change.<b>)</b>	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction

Basic	What is the required practical for rate of reaction? Describe two different methods.	<b>Method 1 — Marble chips + HCl (CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂):</b><br>• Measure mass of flask + contents on balance. CO₂ escapes → mass decreases. Record mass every 30 seconds. Plot mass vs time graph.<br>• OR collect CO₂ in gas syringe — plot volume vs time.<br><b>Method 2 — Sodium thiosulfate + HCl (produces sulphur precipitate — turns cloudy):</b><br>• Draw a cross on paper. Place flask over it. Add HCl. Time until cross disappears from above.<br>• 1/time ∝ rate. Vary: temperature, concentration of Na₂S₂O₃.<br><b>Control variables:</b> volume of solutions, concentration of one reactant, temperature.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction
Basic	How do you calculate rate of reaction from a graph?	Rate = amount of product formed (or reactant used) ÷ time taken.<br><b>From a graph:</b> rate at any point = <b>gradient of the tangent</b> to the curve at that point.<br><b>Initial rate</b> = gradient at time zero (steepest part of curve — start of reaction).<br><b>Units:</b> g/s (loss of mass method), cm³/s (gas volume method).<br><b>As reaction proceeds:</b> gradient decreases as reactants are used up → rate decreases. Curve levels off when reaction is complete (one reactant fully used).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction
Basic	[HT only] What are the Haber process conditions and why are these specific conditions chosen?	<b>Reaction:</b> N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (ΔH = −92 kJ/mol — exothermic, fewer gas molecules on right)<br><b>Conditions:</b> temperature ~450°C, pressure ~200 atm, iron catalyst.<br><b>Temperature compromise:</b> lower T → higher yield (Le Chatelier: exothermic, cool favours products) BUT too slow. 450°C balances reasonable yield with acceptable rate.<br><b>Pressure:</b> higher P → higher yield (4 mol gas → 2 mol gas, high pressure favours fewer moles). 200 atm is economic limit — higher would require stronger/more expensive equipment.<br><b>Catalyst:</b> iron catalyst increases rate without affecting equilibrium position — recycled unreacted N₂ and H₂. Basic	How does surface area affect rate of reaction? Give an example.	Increasing surface area increases rate because there are more particles exposed at the surface — more collisions per second with the other reactant.<br><b>Example:</b> powdered CaCO₃ reacts faster with HCl than marble chips of the same mass — same number of moles, but powder has far greater total surface area.<br><b>Practical evidence:</b> in the marble chips/HCl required practical, using powder instead of chips → steeper gradient on mass vs time graph → faster initial rate, same total mass loss.<br><b>Relevant to explosions:</b> fine dust (flour, coal) has enormous surface area → can explode rapidly when ignited. Basic	What are the three methods of measuring the rate of a reaction?	<b>1. Loss of mass:</b> reaction produces a gas that escapes. Weigh flask on balance at regular intervals. Rate = gradient of mass vs time graph. E.g. CaCO₃ + HCl → CO₂ escapes.<br><b>2. Volume of gas produced:</b> collect gas in a gas syringe or inverted measuring cylinder over water. Plot volume vs time. E.g. H₂O₂ decomposition → O₂ collected.<br><b>3. Colour change / turbidity:</b> measure time for a colour change or cloudiness to develop. E.g. Na₂S₂O₃ + HCl → yellow sulphur precipitate; time until cross disappears. Rate ∝ 1/time.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Rate of Reaction
