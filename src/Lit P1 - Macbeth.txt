Basic	Why are soliloquies significant in Macbeth specifically?	Soliloquies give the audience privileged access to Macbeth's true thoughts — we see the gap between his public behaviour and private mind. His first aside ('why do I yield to that suggestion') shows murderous thoughts BEFORE Lady Macbeth even reads the letter — proving he is not simply 'manipulated' by her but already capable of evil. This is crucial: the witches and Lady Macbeth expose his flaw but don't create it.	English Lit::Paper 1::Macbeth

Shakespeare uses this to create dramatic irony — Macbeth sees Banquo as a {{c4::threat}} when he is simply a loyal friend.		English Lit::Paper 1::Macbeth
Basic	How does setting function in Macbeth?	The heath opening establishes a dark, gothic tone — suggesting free will is contested and nature will restore order despite human interference. Castles signal the high status of characters against their low-status actions (dramatic irony). The battlefield setting at both opening and close provides cyclical structure — reminding audiences of Macbeth's true identity as a celebrated soldier. The play begins and ends with war, but the first is righteous and the second corrective.	English Lit::Paper 1::Macbeth
Basic	Why is the dagger in Macbeth theatrically significant?	It is deliberately ambiguous — Shakespeare calls it a 'dagger of the mind' (a hallucination) but its handle points 'toward my hand', tempting action. The command word 'Come' transfers responsibility for the murder to the dagger itself. Directors must decide whether to show it or not — if real, the supernatural world is literally present; if imagined, Macbeth's guilt is already manifesting. Either interpretation enriches the themes.
