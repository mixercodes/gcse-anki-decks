Basic	Who were the Bigods and why did they choose Framlingham?	Roger Bigod I was granted the manor in 1101 by Henry I. Framlingham was already a reasonably sized town (600 people, worth £44 in the Domesday Book). It had a Saxon settlement and was a strategic location. Roger likely built a timber fortress overlooking the mere. The Bigods became Earls of Norfolk — one of the most powerful noble families in England.	History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	Hugh Bigod (1st Earl of Norfolk) made changes to his father's timber fortress: a {{c1::stone chamber block}} with {{c2::2}} storeys, {{c3::fireplaces and stone chimneys}} (2 survive today), a large {{c4::timber hall}} for feasting, and a {{c5::chapel}}. The {{c6::chapel window}} in the curtain wall still survives. Hugh later sided with {{c7::Empress Matilda}} against King Stephen, so Henry II had the castle destroyed in {{c8::1175}}.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	Roger Bigod II rebuilt Framlingham from {{c1::1189}} under King Richard I. He built the massive {{c2::stone curtain wall}} we see today. In {{c3::1213}} he hosted King John at the castle. He was among the barons who forced King John to sign the {{c4::Magna Carta}} in {{c5::1215}}. As punishment, King John {{c6::besieged}} Framlingham and seized it in {{c7::1216}}. Basic	What are the key physical features of the Bigod curtain wall at Framlingham?	Built c.1190. Stands 10.5m high and 2.3m thick. Built from local flint and septaria stone. 13 towers rising 3.8m above the wall walk — backless (no enclosed interior). Arrow loops for archers. Holes for wooden shutters. Crenellations at the top. Wall walk wide enough for two people to pass.	History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Basic	What are the key defensive features Roger Bigod II added to Framlingham?	Two deep dry ditches around the castle (not water-filled) — to prevent tunnelling and make the walls look more intimidating. Gatehouse with drawbridge and portcullis (portcullis slot still visible; actual portcullis gone). Well 30m deep near the gate — essential for withstanding a siege. All visible from the castle today. Cloze	Framlingham's {{c1::well}} is {{c2::30m}} deep and located near the gate. It was essential in times of {{c3::siege}} for fresh water and sanitation. The {{c4::gatehouse}} still shows the {{c5::portcullis slot}} above the gate-tower, but the portcullis itself is {{c6::gone}}. The latrines expelled waste down chutes through the curtain wall — an example survives on {{c7::Tower 13}}.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	The Great Hall was built by {{c1::Roger Bigod II}} for dining and entertaining, including {{c2::King John}} in 1213. It was located where the {{c3::poorhouse}} building now stands and would have been open to the roof with an open {{c4::hearth}}. Today only {{c5::wooden beam holes}} in the curtain wall survive to show where it stood.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Basic	How did the Howard family come to own Framlingham and what changes did they make?	John Howard was made Duke of Norfolk in 1483 by Richard III. After the Battle of Bosworth (1485) the castle briefly passed to John de Vere. Thomas Howard I recovered it from 1489, becoming a leading Tudor courtier. The Howards added: decorative brick chimneys (both functional and fake, imitating Hampton Court style), wider gatehouse, red brick buildings, a bridge to pleasure gardens. Thomas Howard I died at Framlingham aged 80 in 1524.	History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	The Howard chimneys at Framlingham were built to imitate the style of {{c1::Hampton Court}}, Henry VIII's palace. Some chimneys are {{c2::functional}} (attached to fireplaces) and some are {{c3::fake}} (built purely for show). This shows the Howards were keen to display {{c4::wealth and power}} and their links to {{c5::Henry VIII}}. Thomas Howard promoted two nieces as Henry's wives: {{c6::Anne Boleyn}} (2nd wife) and {{c7::Catherine Howard}} (5th wife) — both were beheaded.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	In {{c1::1552}}, Framlingham was given to {{c2::Mary Tudor}} in Henry VIII's will. When Edward VI died in {{c3::1553}}, he tried to make {{c4::Lady Jane Grey}} queen. Mary fled to Framlingham and on {{c5::12 July 1553}} raised her standard and rallied her troops. By {{c6::19 July}} the Privy Council accepted her as queen. She was the first {{c7::Queen Regnant}} of England.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Basic	How did Framlingham change under Elizabeth I (1558–1603)?	The fourth Howard duke (Elizabeth's relative through Anne Boleyn) was initially the premier nobleman. But he was executed in 1572 for plotting to marry Mary Queen of Scots. Framlingham passed back to the Crown. By 1580 the Great Park was turned into fields. From 1580 the castle was used as a prison — by 1600 it held 40 Roman Catholic priests and recusants. A 1589 survey estimated £100 of urgent repairs were needed.	History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Cloze	Sir {{c1::Robert Hitcham}} bought Framlingham in {{c2::1635}} for £{{c3::14,000}}. He came from humble origins but became a lawyer to {{c4::James I}} and an MP for Suffolk. In his will he instructed that 'all the castle, saving the {{c5::stone walls}}, be pulled down' and a {{c6::poorhouse}} built. Pembroke College, {{c7::Cambridge}} was left the castle on the condition they carried out his wishes.		History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
Basic	What happened to the poorhouse at Framlingham (1635–1839)?	Hitcham died 1636; legal wrangling delayed action. Almshouses built 1654; Red House erected soon after. 1666: plague struck Framlingham; castle buildings used as isolation wards. Funds mismanaged; poorhouse closed temporarily. 1699: failed attempt to re-establish poorhouse for children. 1729: new poorhouse built on site of medieval Great Hall with parish funds. Housed adults and children until 1839, when poor were moved to Wickham Market workhouse. Building became a parish hall — now a tourist attraction run by English Heritage. Basic	How is Framlingham typical or atypical compared to other castles?	Typical: curtain wall of local flint and septaria stone similar to Orford Castle (12 miles away, also built c.1166–73); both built as baronial power bases; both had ditches and gatehouses. Atypical: Framlingham has no keep (shell keep or stone tower) — unusual for a Norman castle; it has 13 backless towers (no enclosed rooms). Unlike many castles, Framlingham never fell out of use — it served as castle, prison, and poorhouse continuously.	History::Paper 3::Framlingham Castle
