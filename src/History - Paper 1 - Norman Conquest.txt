Basic	What were the key features of Anglo-Saxon England before 1066?	Towns defended by royal burhs (fortified mud walls). Women had legal rights — could own land, claim fines for harassment, and divorce. Anglo-Saxons were known for engraving and metalwork (e.g. Alfred Jewel, Fuller Brooch). The Church used English not Latin; Archbishop Stigand practised pluralism (held multiple posts) — the Pope was unhappy.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest

Three other claimants: {{c5::Harald Hardrada}} (Norway), {{c6::Edgar the Atheling}} (blood-relative), and {{c7::William Duke of Normandy}}.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest

Basic	How did William win the Battle of Hastings (14 October 1066)?	Harold's army was exhausted after marching south from Stamford Bridge. William used the feigned retreat trick — Normans pretended to flee, drawing English off the hill. Normans had stirrups, which allowed knights to fight effectively on horseback. Harold was killed (arrow in the eye or cut down by knights — shown in the Bayeux Tapestry). The English shield wall broke after hours of fighting.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Cloze	After Hastings, William waited two weeks for the English to {{c1::surrender}}. Instead, the Witan elected {{c2::Edgar the Atheling}} as king. William decided to take control by force. He was crowned at {{c3::Westminster}} on {{c4::25 December 1066}}.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Basic	What happened during January–March 1067 (establishing control)?	William wanted to calm the situation. He took land from English lords but let some buy it back. He built castles across East Anglia and gave them to loyal followers: William FitzOsbern in Norwich, Odo (Bishop of Bayeux) in the south-east at Dover. Then William returned to Normandy, taking Edwin, Morcar and Edgar with him as insurance.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest

Basic	How did the Normans change castles in England?	Built many more — especially motte-and-bailey (wooden keep on a raised mound, surrounded by a bailey). Later replaced with stone. Key castles: Tower of London (started 1075–79), Castle Acre, Old Sarum. Castles served as military bases and symbols of Norman power and control.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Cloze	The {{c1::Domesday Book}} was compiled in {{c2::1086}} on William's orders. It was a survey of all land, owners and values in England. By this date, only {{c3::5%}} of land was still owned by Englishmen. It showed William how much {{c4::tax}} he could collect.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Cloze	New Norman laws: {{c1::Murdrum Fine}} — if a Norman was killed and the killer not found, the whole {{c2::local community}} was fined. {{c3::Forest Law}} — William claimed all forests as royal hunting land; poaching meant death. {{c4::Trial by combat}} replaced Anglo-Saxon oaths.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Cloze	William replaced Archbishop {{c1::Stigand}} with {{c2::Lanfranc}}, a much stricter Norman archbishop. The Pope {{c3::approved}} the Conquest. William began rebuilding cathedrals — {{c4::Winchester, York, Canterbury, Ely}}. By 1087, only {{c5::10%}} of people lived in towns.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Basic	What were the main rebellions against William after 1066 and who led them?	Edric the Wild (Mercia, 1067). Gytha's rebellion (Exeter, 1068). Northern rebellions (1068–69) — Edwin, Morcar, Edgar the Atheling. Danish-backed revolt and Harrying of the North (1069–70). Hereward the Wake at Ely (1070–71). All were eventually crushed, with the North suffering most.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Basic	Who were the key individuals in the Norman Conquest and what roles did they play?	Harold Godwinson: English king, killed at Hastings. Harald Hardrada: Viking claimant, killed at Stamford Bridge. Tostig: Harold's traitorous brother, killed at Stamford Bridge. William of Normandy: conqueror, became king. Edgar the Atheling: blood-claim but young, eventually submitted. Hereward the Wake: last rebel at Ely. Lanfranc: new Norman Archbishop, stricter than Stigand.	History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Cloze	The Bayeux Tapestry shows Harold's death as an {{c1::arrow in the eye}} or being {{c2::cut down by Norman knights}}. It was made by the {{c3::Normans}} and so presents a {{c4::Norman perspective}} on the Conquest.		History::Paper 1::Norman Conquest
Basic	What were the positive and negative changes brought by the Norman Conquest?	Positive: beautiful stone churches and cathedrals built; Domesday Book created a clear record of land ownership; more organised feudal system. Negative: English lost their land and language; Harrying caused mass starvation in the North; harsh laws like Murdrum Fine and Forest Law; English culture (language, engraving) suppressed.
