Cloze	Relative formula mass (Mr): sum of relative atomic masses (Ar) of all atoms in the formula. E.g. H₂O: (2×{{c1::1}}) + {{c2::16}} = {{c3::18}}. CO₂: 12 + (2×16) = {{c4::44}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Cloze	1 mole of a substance contains {{c1::6.02 × 10²³}} particles (Avogadro's constant). Moles = {{c2::mass (g)}} ÷ {{c3::Mr}}. If Mr of NaCl = 58.5 and you have 11.7g: moles = 11.7÷58.5 = {{c4::0.2 mol}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Basic	What is percentage yield and why is it rarely 100%?	% yield = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100. Never 100% because: reaction may be reversible (doesn't go to completion), some product lost during separation/purification, side reactions occur.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Basic	What is atom economy and why does it matter?	Atom economy = (Mr of desired product ÷ total Mr of all products) × 100. Measures how much reactant mass ends up in the desired product. High atom economy = less waste = more sustainable and cost-effective.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Cloze	The law of conservation of mass: mass of {{c1::reactants}} = mass of {{c2::products}}. If mass appears to change in an experiment, it is because a {{c3::gas}} has been produced or has been used from the air (e.g. burning magnesium gains mass from {{c4::oxygen}}). Basic	Worked example: reacting masses from a balanced equation. What mass of water is produced from 4g of hydrogen? (2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O)	Step 1: Mr of H₂ = 2, Mr of H₂O = 18.<br>Step 2: From equation, 2 mol H₂ → 2 mol H₂O (1:1 ratio).<br>Step 3: moles of H₂ = 4 ÷ 2 = 2 mol.<br>Step 4: moles of H₂O = 2 mol.<br>Step 5: mass of H₂O = 2 × 18 = <b>36 g</b>.<br><b>Shortcut:</b> mass ratio from equation = 4:36. Scale to given mass: 4g H₂ → 36g H₂O.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Cloze	Concentration in mol/dm³ = {{c1::moles of solute}} ÷ {{c2::volume of solution in dm³}}. 1 dm³ = {{c3::1000 cm³}}. To convert cm³ to dm³, {{c4::divide by 1000}}. Example: 0.5 mol NaCl in 2 dm³ → concentration = {{c5::0.25 mol/dm³}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Basic	How do you calculate concentration in g/dm³ and convert between g/dm³ and mol/dm³?	<b>Concentration (g/dm³) = mass of solute (g) ÷ volume of solution (dm³)</b><br>To convert mol/dm³ → g/dm³: multiply by Mr.<br>To convert g/dm³ → mol/dm³: divide by Mr.<br><b>Example:</b> 0.25 mol/dm³ NaCl (Mr = 58.5): concentration in g/dm³ = 0.25 × 58.5 = 14.625 g/dm³. Basic	[HT only] Worked example: calculate the concentration of NaOH, given 25.0 cm³ of NaOH required 23.50 cm³ of 0.100 mol/dm³ HCl for neutralisation. (HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O)	Step 1: moles of HCl = 0.100 × (23.50 ÷ 1000) = 0.00235 mol.<br>Step 2: ratio HCl:NaOH = 1:1, so moles NaOH = 0.00235 mol.<br>Step 3: concentration NaOH = 0.00235 ÷ (25.0 ÷ 1000) = <b>0.094 mol/dm³</b>.<br><b>Exam tip:</b> always convert cm³ to dm³ (÷1000) before using in concentration formula. Basic	[HT only] What is a limiting reactant? How do you identify it?	The limiting reactant is the one that is completely used up first — it limits the amount of product that can form. The other reactant is in excess (some remains at the end).<br><b>Method:</b> calculate moles of each reactant → compare to the equation ratio → the one that runs out first is limiting.<br><b>Example:</b> 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O. 3 mol H₂ and 2 mol O₂: need 1.5 mol O₂ for 3 mol H₂ → O₂ is in excess. H₂ is limiting.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Basic	Worked example: percentage yield. Theoretical yield of NaCl = 5.85g. Actual yield from experiment = 4.68g. What is the percentage yield?	% yield = (actual yield ÷ theoretical yield) × 100<br>= (4.68 ÷ 5.85) × 100 = <b>80%</b><br><b>Reasons yield is less than 100%:</b> reaction may be reversible (not complete), product lost during filtering/evaporation/transfer, side reactions producing different products. Cloze	(Higher Tier) Molar volume of a gas at room temperature and pressure (RTP): 1 mole of any gas = {{c1::24 dm³}} (24,000 cm³). Volume (dm³) = moles × {{c2::24}}. Moles = volume (dm³) ÷ {{c3::24}}. Example: 2g H₂ (Mr=2) = 1 mol → volume = {{c4::24 dm³}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Quantitative Chemistry
Basic	(Higher Tier) Calculate the volume of CO₂ produced when 10g of CaCO₃ reacts with excess HCl. (CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂, Mr CaCO₃ = 100)	Step 1: moles CaCO₃ = 10 ÷ 100 = 0.1 mol.<br>Step 2: from equation, 1 mol CaCO₃ → 1 mol CO₂ (1:1 ratio).<br>Step 3: moles CO₂ = 0.1 mol.<br>Step 4: volume CO₂ = 0.1 × 24 = <b>2.4 dm³</b> (or 2400 cm³).<br><b>Remember:</b> only works at room temperature and pressure (RTP). Basic	What is the required practical for Quantitative Chemistry? What is the method for an acid-alkali titration?	<b>Method:</b> fill burette with acid. Pipette 25.0 cm³ of alkali into conical flask. Add a few drops of indicator (phenolphthalein — pink in alkali, colourless in acid). Run acid from burette slowly, swirling constantly. Stop when indicator just changes colour (endpoint). Record volume added (titre).<br><b>Repeat</b> to get concordant results (within 0.10 cm³).<br><b>Mean titre</b> = average of concordant results (exclude anomalies).<br><b>Accuracy tip:</b> rinse burette with acid before filling; use white tile to see colour change clearly.
