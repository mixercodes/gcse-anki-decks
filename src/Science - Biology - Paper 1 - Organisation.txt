Basic	What are the four main tissue types in animals?	Epithelial tissue (covers surfaces), muscle tissue (contracts for movement), connective tissue (supports and connects), glandular tissue (secretes substances — e.g. enzymes, hormones).	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	What is the role of the digestive system and name the enzymes involved?	Breaks down large insoluble food molecules into small soluble ones for absorption. Amylase: breaks down starch → simple sugars (made in salivary glands, pancreas, small intestine). Protease: proteins → amino acids (stomach, pancreas, small intestine). Lipase: lipids → fatty acids + glycerol (pancreas, small intestine).	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Cloze	The stomach produces {{c1::hydrochloric acid}} (pH {{c2::2}}) to kill bacteria and provide optimum pH for {{c3::protease (pepsin)}}. The small intestine is neutralised by {{c4::bile}} produced in the {{c5::liver}} and stored in the {{c6::gallbladder}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	How is the small intestine adapted for absorption?	Villi increase surface area. Microvilli (on villi) further increase surface area. Rich blood supply maintains concentration gradient. Thin walls (one cell thick) = short diffusion distance. Lacteals absorb fatty acids and glycerol into the lymphatic system.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	What is the structure of the heart and how does blood flow through it?	Right side receives deoxygenated blood from body (vena cava) → pumps to lungs (pulmonary artery). Left side receives oxygenated blood from lungs (pulmonary vein) → pumps to body (aorta). Four chambers: right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, left ventricle. Valves prevent backflow.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Cloze	Coronary heart disease: {{c1::fatty deposits}} build up in the coronary arteries, restricting blood flow to the heart muscle. Treated by: {{c2::statins}} (reduce cholesterol), {{c3::stents}} (keep arteries open), {{c4::bypass surgery}} (new vessel grafted around blockage).		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	What is cancer and what are the differences between benign and malignant tumours?	Cancer: uncontrolled cell division. Benign: grows in one place, doesn't spread, can be removed. Malignant: invades neighbouring tissues and can metastasise (spread through blood/lymph to other parts of the body). Risk factors: smoking, UV radiation, viral infection, genetics.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Cloze	Plants have: {{c1::meristem tissue}} (growth at root and shoot tips), {{c2::xylem}} (transports water and minerals UP — dead cells, lignin walls), {{c3::phloem}} (transports sugars UP and DOWN — living cells). Transpiration pulls water up through {{c4::stomata}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Cloze	Blood components: {{c1::red blood cells}} (carry O₂ via haemoglobin, no nucleus), {{c2::white blood cells}} (immune defence — phagocytes and lymphocytes), {{c3::platelets}} (cell fragments, trigger blood clotting), {{c4::plasma}} (liquid component carrying CO₂, hormones, glucose, urea, antibodies, proteins). Basic	What are the adaptations of the red blood cell and why are they suited to its function?	<b>Biconcave disc shape:</b> increased surface area for O₂ absorption and diffusion.<br><b>No nucleus:</b> more space for haemoglobin molecules → can carry more O₂.<br><b>Contains haemoglobin:</b> binds O₂ in lungs (oxyhaemoglobin), releases it in tissues where O₂ concentration is low.<br><b>Small and flexible:</b> can squeeze through narrow capillaries (5–8 μm wide).<br><b>Exam tip:</b> haemoglobin + O₂ ⇌ oxyhaemoglobin (reversible, direction depends on O₂ concentration).	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	What are the factors affecting the rate of transpiration and how would you investigate them?	<b>Factors that increase transpiration rate:</b> higher temperature (more evaporation), lower humidity (steeper concentration gradient), higher light intensity (stomata open wider), higher wind speed (removes water vapour — steeper gradient).<br><b>Investigation — potometer:</b> measures water uptake (proxy for transpiration). Bubble moves along capillary tube as water is absorbed. Change one variable at a time (temperature/humidity/wind/light) and measure rate of bubble movement.<br><b>Key point:</b> guard cells open stomata in light (for photosynthesis) → most transpiration occurs during the day.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	How do plants transport water and minerals? What is the transpiration stream?	<b>Route:</b> water enters by osmosis through root hair cells → moves through root by osmosis → enters xylem vessels → moves up stem by transpiration pull → exits through stomata in leaves as water vapour.<br><b>Xylem:</b> dead, hollow cells with lignified walls forming continuous tubes. Lignin is waterproof — prevents water loss through walls. No end walls — continuous column of water.<br><b>Transpiration pull:</b> evaporation from leaves creates tension → pulls water up from roots. Works because water molecules are cohesive (stick together) — cohesion-tension model.<br><b>Mineral ions</b> dissolved in water travel with it.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Organisation
Basic	What are the required practicals for Biology Paper 1? Describe the food tests.	<b>Food tests (required practical):</b><br>• <b>Starch:</b> add iodine solution → blue/black colour indicates starch present.<br>• <b>Glucose (reducing sugars):</b> add Benedict's solution + heat → colour changes from blue to brick-red/orange if reducing sugar present.<br>• <b>Protein (Biuret test):</b> add NaOH then CuSO₄ → turns purple/lilac if protein present.<br>• <b>Fat (ethanol emulsion test):</b> dissolve food in ethanol, pour into water → white emulsion/cloudy if fat present.<br><b>Control:</b> test with distilled water to confirm reagents don't give false positive.
