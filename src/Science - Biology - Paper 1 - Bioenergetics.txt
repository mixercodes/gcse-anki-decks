Cloze	{{c1::Metabolism}} is the sum of all {{c2::chemical reactions}} in a cell or the body. It includes {{c3::respiration}} (breaking down glucose to transfer energy), {{c4::protein synthesis}} (from amino acids), and {{c5::photosynthesis}} (plants building glucose). The energy transferred by respiration is used to {{c6::build larger molecules}}, {{c7::contract muscles}}, {{c8::maintain body temperature}} and transmit nerve impulses. Basic	What are the factors that limit the rate of photosynthesis?	Light intensity, CO₂ concentration, temperature, and chlorophyll concentration. Increasing any one of these increases the rate until another factor becomes limiting. At high temperatures the enzymes controlling photosynthesis denature.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Basic	How do plants use glucose?	Respiration (energy for growth). Made into starch for storage. Made into cellulose for cell walls. Made into lipids (oils/fats) for storage. Combined with nitrates to make amino acids and proteins.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Cloze	Aerobic respiration equation: {{c1::C₆H₁₂O₆}} + {{c2::6O₂}} → {{c3::6CO₂}} + {{c4::6H₂O}} (+ energy). Occurs in the {{c5::mitochondria}}. Releases {{c6::more}} energy than anaerobic respiration.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Cloze	Anaerobic respiration in animals: {{c1::glucose}} → {{c2::lactic acid}} (+ small amount of energy). Causes {{c3::oxygen debt}} — lactic acid must be oxidised after exercise. In plants/yeast: glucose → {{c4::ethanol}} + {{c5::CO₂}} (fermentation).		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Basic	What happens to the body during exercise?	Heart rate, breathing rate and breath volume increase to deliver more O₂ and glucose to muscles and remove CO₂. Muscles respire aerobically then anaerobically when O₂ can't be delivered fast enough. Lactic acid builds up causing muscle fatigue. After exercise, oxygen is used to break down the lactic acid — this is why breathing rate stays elevated after stopping.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Basic	How do you investigate the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis (pondweed / Elodea)?	<b>Method:</b> place pondweed in sodium hydrogen carbonate solution (provides CO₂). Shine a lamp at different distances. Count bubbles per minute (or measure volume of O₂ collected using a gas syringe) at each distance.<br><b>Light intensity</b> ∝ 1/distance² (inverse square law) — double the distance → quarter the light intensity.<br><b>Control variables:</b> temperature (use water bath), CO₂ concentration (sodium hydrogen carbonate keeps constant), same piece of pondweed.<br><b>Expected result:</b> as light intensity increases, rate increases (up to the point where CO₂ or temperature becomes limiting).	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Basic	Draw and interpret limiting factor graphs for photosynthesis.	<b>Light intensity graph:</b> rate increases then levels off — at plateau, light is no longer limiting (CO₂ or temperature now limits).<br><b>CO₂ concentration graph:</b> increases then plateaus — at high CO₂, another factor limits.<br><b>Temperature graph:</b> increases up to ~40°C then sharply decreases — enzymes denature above optimum (not a simple plateau).<br><b>Key exam point:</b> to determine what is limiting at any point on the graph — the factor that makes the plateau rise when increased is the one that was limiting. If light increases but rate doesn't — something else is limiting.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
Cloze	The word equation for aerobic respiration: {{c1::glucose}} + {{c2::oxygen}} → {{c3::carbon dioxide}} + {{c4::water}} (+ energy). For anaerobic in mammals: glucose → {{c5::lactic acid}} (+ small amount of energy). In yeast: glucose → {{c6::ethanol}} + {{c7::carbon dioxide}}. Anaerobic produces far {{c8::less}} ATP than aerobic.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Bioenergetics
