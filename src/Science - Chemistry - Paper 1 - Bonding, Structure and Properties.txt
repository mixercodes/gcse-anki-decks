Basic	What is ionic bonding?	Transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal. Metal loses electrons → positive ion (cation). Non-metal gains electrons → negative ion (anion). Ions arranged in a giant ionic lattice held together by strong electrostatic forces. High melting points. Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions free to move). Basic	What is covalent bonding?	Sharing of electron pairs between non-metal atoms. Each shared pair = one covalent bond. Can form simple molecular structures (low melting points, don't conduct electricity) or giant covalent structures (very high melting points, e.g. diamond, graphite, silicon dioxide). Cloze	Diamond: each carbon forms {{c1::4}} covalent bonds in a {{c2::giant covalent lattice}}. Very hard, high melting point, {{c3::doesn't conduct electricity}} (no free electrons). Graphite: each carbon forms {{c4::3}} bonds in layers — has {{c5::delocalised electrons}} and {{c6::conducts electricity}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Bonding, Structure and Properties
Cloze	Graphene: a single layer of {{c1::graphite}} — one atom thick. Excellent {{c2::electrical conductor}}. Fullerenes (e.g. {{c3::Buckminsterfullerene C₆₀}}): carbon atoms arranged in hollow {{c4::spheres or tubes}}. Used in drug delivery and nanotechnology. Basic	What is metallic bonding?	Lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a sea of delocalised (free) electrons. Strong electrostatic attraction between ions and electrons. This explains: high melting points (strong bonds), electrical conductivity (free electrons carry charge), malleability (layers of ions can slide — electrons maintain bonding), thermal conductivity. Basic	What are alloys and why are they harder than pure metals?	Alloys are mixtures of metals (or a metal with another element). Different-sized atoms disrupt the regular lattice, making it harder for layers to slide over each other. Examples: steel (iron + carbon), brass (copper + zinc), bronze (copper + tin). E.g. water, CO₂, ammonia.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Bonding, Structure and Properties
Basic	What are polymers and how are they formed?	Long chain molecules made by joining many small monomer units in polymerisation. Monomers must have a double bond (alkenes). Many polymers come from crude oil. Properties depend on the monomer and conditions used. Generally: strong, chemically unreactive, solid at room temperature, don't conduct electricity. Basic	What are nanoparticles and why are they different from bulk materials?	Nanoparticles are particles with dimensions between 1 and 100 nanometres (1 nm = 10⁻⁹ m). At this scale, the proportion of surface atoms is very large compared to bulk material.<br><b>Why different:</b> extremely large surface area to volume ratio → greatly enhanced reactivity and different properties. Quantum effects also become significant.<br><b>Uses:</b> silver nanoparticles (antibacterial — socks, wound dressings), titanium oxide (sunscreen), carbon nanotubes (strong lightweight materials, electronics), gold nanoparticles (targeted drug delivery, cancer treatment).<br><b>Concerns:</b> very small size means they can penetrate cell membranes and accumulate in tissues — long-term health effects unknown.
