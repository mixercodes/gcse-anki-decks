Basic	What is the difference between potable water and pure water?	Pure water contains only H₂O molecules. Potable water is safe to drink but may contain dissolved minerals — it is not 100% pure. Treatment of fresh water: screening → sedimentation → filtration → chlorination. Desalination (for seawater): distillation or reverse osmosis — both energy-intensive. Basic	What are the four stages of sewage treatment?	1. Screening — removes large solids. 2. Primary treatment — sedimentation, sludge settles. 3. Secondary treatment — aeration tank, aerobic bacteria break down organic matter. 4. Sludge digestion — anaerobic bacteria produce biogas; dried sludge used as fertiliser. Basic	What is the life cycle assessment (LCA)?	Evaluates the environmental impact of a product across its entire life: raw material extraction, manufacturing, use, and disposal. Considers energy use, water use, waste production and pollution at each stage. Used to compare products and make more sustainable choices. Can be biased depending on assumptions made. Cloze	Finite resources: {{c1::fossil fuels}}, metal ores — cannot be replaced on a human timescale. Renewable resources: {{c2::wood}}, {{c3::plant-based materials}} — can be replaced. Recycling metals saves {{c4::energy}} and reduces need to mine new ore (reduces habitat destruction).		Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Using Resources
Basic	What are the environmental advantages of recycling metals compared to extracting from ores?	<b>Advantages of recycling:</b><br>• Much less energy required — e.g. recycling aluminium uses only ~5% of the energy needed for extraction from bauxite by electrolysis.<br>• Reduces mining → less habitat destruction, less toxic waste from mining operations.<br>• Reduces CO₂ emissions (less fossil fuel burned for energy).<br>• Conserves finite ore reserves.<br>• Reduces landfill waste.<br><b>Challenges:</b> sorting different metals is time-consuming and expensive. Some alloys are difficult to separate. Some contamination from other materials.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Using Resources
Basic	What is the Haber process for making ammonia and how is it related to using resources sustainably?	N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (450°C, 200 atm, iron catalyst)<br><b>Sustainability issues:</b> nitrogen comes from air (unlimited). Hydrogen comes from methane (natural gas) — finite resource that produces CO₂.<br><b>Alternative:</b> electrolysis of water using renewable electricity to produce hydrogen — but currently expensive.<br><b>Ammonia use:</b> mainly for making <b>nitrogen fertilisers</b> (e.g. ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate) → increases crop yields → supports global population. However, excess fertiliser → eutrophication of water.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Using Resources
