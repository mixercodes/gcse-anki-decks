January 1933: Hitler appointed Chancellor.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Basic	How did the Nazis control Germany through fear?	SS (Schutzstaffel): Hitler's personal bodyguard and elite force — ran concentration camps. SD (Sicherheitsdienst): Nazi intelligence service. Gestapo: secret state police — used informers and torture; 26% of cases came from public denunciations. People's Court: dealt with political crimes; unfair trials. Concentration camps: first opened March 1933; held political opponents.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Despite Nazi propaganda suggesting agents were {{c4::everywhere}}, the reality was that Germans {{c5::informed on each other}} voluntarily.		History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany
Basic	How did the Nazis control Germany through propaganda?	Goebbels ran the Ministry of Propaganda. Controlled: all radio stations (taken over 1934), newspapers, cinema, art, music, theatre, school curriculum. Rallies like Nuremberg used spectacle and emotion. Public loudspeakers broadcast Hitler's speeches in workplaces, shops and pubs. Key message: national pride, anti-Semitism, racial purity.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Edelweiss Pirates: youth groups who fought Hitler Youth and sang anti-Nazi songs — not organised enough to threaten the regime.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany
Basic	What did the Nazis want from German workers, and what was the reality?	Wanted: full employment, loyalty, productivity. Did: Strength Through Joy (cheap holidays, leisure), Beauty of Labour (improve workplaces), abolish trade unions. Results: unemployment fell from 6 million (1933) to near zero (1938). BUT: workers had no rights, could not strike, wages were controlled. Standard of living for workers was mixed.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Basic	How did the Nazis control young people through education and youth groups?	Schools: all teachers had to join the National Socialist Teachers League (1933); curriculum pushed Nazi ideology in every subject (e.g. maths problems about racial 'impurity'). Youth groups: Hitler Youth (boys 10–18) and League of German Maidens (BDM, girls) — cheap holidays, hikes, sports, Nazi songs. Membership was compulsory from 1936. Some children informed on parents to the Gestapo.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Basic	What were the stages of Nazi persecution of Jews leading to the Final Solution?	1933–38: boycotts, civil service sackings, Nuremberg Laws. 1938: Kristallnacht. First solution (1938–39): forced emigration. Second solution (1939–41): concentration in ghettos (e.g. Warsaw). 1941: Einsatzgruppen (mobile killing units) followed the army into the Soviet Union. 1942: Wannsee Conference planned the Final Solution. Death camps set up in Eastern Europe (e.g. Auschwitz, Treblinka).	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Initially positive: Germany gained food, raw materials from conquered territory. 2/5 Germans actually ate better due to rationing. Luxury goods imported (mostly to senior Nazis). But: coal shortages in winter 1940; rationing queues; children evacuated; up to 4 air raids a week.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany

Basic	Who opposed the Nazis during World War II and how effective were they?	White Rose (Munich University, 1942–43): Hans and Sophie Scholl distributed anti-Nazi leaflets; arrested and executed February 1943. July Bomb Plot (1944): senior military officers tried to assassinate Hitler; failed; 5,000 executed. Rosenstrasse (February 1943): German women protested for release of Jewish husbands; succeeded. Cardinal Galen (1941): publicly condemned Nazi euthanasia programme. Passive resistance: absenteeism, spreading rumours, listening to BBC.	History::Paper 2::Life in Nazi Germany
Basic	How did the Nazis treat occupied Poland and the Netherlands differently?	Poland: first country occupied (1939); 500,000 Germans moved in, Poles moved out; Hans Frank ruled by terror; 1.9 million Poles killed; 3 of 3.5 million Polish Jews murdered; extermination camps built. Netherlands (occupied May 1940): Dutch seen as Aryan, so no mass murder; no German resettlement; but Dutch Jews still deported 1943; 500,000 Dutch workers sent to Germany as forced labour.
