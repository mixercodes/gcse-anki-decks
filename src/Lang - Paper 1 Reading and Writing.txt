Basic	What is the structure of AQA English Language Paper 1?	Section A: Reading — one literary fiction extract, 4 questions. Q1 (4 marks): list 4 things. Q2 (8 marks): language analysis. Q3 (8 marks): structural analysis. Q4 (20 marks): evaluate and comment on writer's methods. Section B: Writing — one creative writing task (narrative or description), 40 marks (24 content, 16 technical accuracy).	English Lang::Paper 1
Cloze	Paper 1 Q2 ({{c1::8}} marks) asks you to analyse {{c2::language}}. Paper 1 Q3 ({{c3::8}} marks) asks you to analyse {{c4::structure}}. Paper 1 Q4 ({{c5::20}} marks) asks you to {{c6::evaluate}} a statement about the text.		English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	How should you structure a Paper 1 Q2 language analysis paragraph?	Identify the technique/word class. Quote specifically (zoom in on one word ideally). Analyse the connotations/effect. Link to the writer's intent. Use the word 'suggests', 'implies', 'conveys' rather than 'shows'. Aim for 3-4 developed points. Never just identify a technique — always analyse the effect.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What are the five ways to use word choice effectively in creative writing?	A — Tone: word choice creates a specific mood or atmosphere. B — Sound: alliteration, sibilance, plosives, onomatopoeia create sonic effects. C — Syllable: rhythm and pace controlled by word length (monosyllabic = blunt/fast, polysyllabic = flowing/slow). D — Sentence/punctuation: word placed at end of sentence for impact, short sentences for shock. E — Pattern: semantic fields, motifs, repetition across paragraphs create cohesion.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	How does sentence length create effect in creative/analytical writing?	Short, monosyllabic sentences create shock, tension, finality (e.g. 'Waves hit.'). Long, complex sentences with multiple clauses create flow, overwhelm or breathlessness. Varied sentence length creates rhythm and controls the reader's pace through a text. Fragments (incomplete sentences) create immediacy and disorientation.	English Lang::Paper 1

The verbs 'pushed and pressed' are {{c4::plosive}}, mimicking the physical force of the waves.		English Lang::Paper 1
Cloze	Using word choice for {{c2::tone}}: 'Weary and wounded, the train limped on, its gasping engine drawing {{c1::laboured}} breaths' — personification of the train as injured creates a tone of {{c3::suffering and exhaustion}}. The adjective 'laboured' has connotations of painful effort.		English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	How should you approach Paper 1 Q3 (structural analysis)?	Think about: where the writer starts and ends (what changes?). Focus shifts — zooming in/out, moving between characters, time shifts. Turning points — where does the narrative shift? Sentence/paragraph length changes. Beginnings and endings of paragraphs — what is foregrounded, what is left until last? Always link structural choices to effect on the reader.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What structural techniques should you identify in Q3?	Cyclical structure (ending mirrors opening). In media res (starting mid-action). Narrative perspective shifts. Contrast paragraphing (juxtaposing two settings/ideas). Withholding information (building tension). Foregrounding (placing important element at start). Delayed revelation (revealing key information late). Syntactic parallelism (repeating grammatical structure).	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What is cyclical structure and how does it work?	When the ending of a piece mirrors or echoes its opening — creating a sense of completion or irony. Example from the model: 'Kate's mind worked furiously, imagining the horrors' at the start mirrors 'Kate's mind worked furiously, remembering the joys' at the end — same phrase, opposite emotion. Shows transformation. Very effective for Q3 analysis and something to use in your own writing.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What is syntactic parallelism and what effect does it create?	Repeating the same grammatical structure across sentences or paragraphs. Example: 'His mother, though, had other ideas. His mother, though, decided... His mother, he sighed...' — the repetition builds frustration and emphasis. Creates rhythm, accumulates pressure. Useful in both analysis and writing.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What is contrast paragraphing?	Deliberately juxtaposing two contrasting descriptions, perspectives or settings in adjacent paragraphs. Example: 'The storm raged outside... The fire flickered inside.' Or: 'The usual crowd of commuters stalked across London... An eager mass of tourists raced across London' — same setting, opposite groups, opposite tones. Creates meaning through opposition.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	How should you approach Paper 1 Q4 (evaluation)?	You are given a statement about how a student interpreted the text. You agree/disagree/partly agree using evidence. Structure: state your view clearly, use 3-4 pieces of evidence, analyse the methods, comment on effect. Use evaluative language: 'to a large extent', 'however', 'more convincingly', 'this is supported by'. Always refer to the writer's methods, not just what happens.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	How is Paper 1 Section B creative writing marked?	40 marks total: 24 for content/organisation, 16 for technical accuracy (spelling, punctuation, grammar, vocabulary). You have a choice: either describe a picture prompt OR write a narrative using a given title/opening. Spend 45-50 mins on this including planning time.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What makes a high-level creative writing response for Paper 1?	Varied and deliberate sentence structures (fragments, complex, short for effect). Rich, specific vocabulary with connotations (not just 'big' — use 'looming', 'vast', 'towering'). A clear narrative or descriptive voice. Structural choices that mirror meaning (cyclical, contrast paragraphing). Controlled perspective and tone throughout. Techniques that serve the writing rather than just being ticked off a list.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What structural techniques can you use in your own creative writing?	Cyclical structure (begin and end with same/transformed image). In media res (start mid-action — 'Suddenly he awoke and was running'). Contrast paragraphing (storm outside vs calm inside). Syntactic parallelism (repeated grammatical structures for rhythm/emphasis). Withholding information (reveal key details late). Varying paragraph length for pace.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What vocabulary techniques create Grade 8/9 creative writing?	Use word choice for tone (precise adjectives and verbs, not generic ones). Use word choice for sound (alliteration, sibilance, plosives that mimic the described action). Use word choice for syllable (polysyllabic for flowing description, monosyllabic for shock). Use word choice for pattern (semantic fields that run through the whole piece, creating cohesion).	English Lang::Paper 1

Basic	Give an example of contrast paragraphing technique from the model texts.	'The usual crowd of commuters stalked across London as the sun's nightly death shattered light over them. Set faces were grim — no smiles or joyous eyes. This was commuting hour, and it was hellish. / An eager mass of tourists raced across London as the sun's daily birth patched light over them. Expressive faces were excited — no frowns or tired eyes. This was visiting hour, and it was glorious.' — exact mirror structure, opposite emotions, powerful juxtaposition.	English Lang::Paper 1
Basic	What techniques does the Q5 Market model text use?	Cyclical structure: 'Kate's mind worked furiously, imagining the horrors' mirrors 'Kate's mind worked furiously, remembering the joys' — same phrase, opposite emotion, shows transformation. Contrast paragraphing: palace (contained, sterile) vs marketplace (chaotic, alive). Semantic field of danger (assaulted, smacked, charged, chokehold, winded) vs semantic field of beauty (sparkling, delicate, exotic). Short sentences for impact: 'She could not settle. She could not stand still.' Parallelism: 'all dominating... all eradicating'.	English Lang::Paper 1
