Basic	How does the skin act as a non-specific defence against infection?	The skin forms a continuous physical barrier that prevents pathogens from entering the body. It also produces antimicrobial secretions (including sebum from sebaceous glands) that kill microorganisms on the surface.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How does the respiratory tract defend against pathogens?	The nose contains hairs and mucus that trap pathogens in inhaled air. The trachea and bronchi are lined with ciliated epithelial cells — cilia sweep mucus (and trapped pathogens) upward to the throat, where it is swallowed and destroyed by stomach acid.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How does the stomach defend against pathogens?	The stomach produces hydrochloric acid (pH 2), which kills most pathogens that are swallowed in food, drink, or mucus swept from the respiratory tract.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How does the immune system respond to pathogens?	White blood cells: 1) Phagocytosis — phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens. 2) Antibody production — lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that match antigens on the pathogen, causing clumping and destruction. 3) Antitoxin production — neutralises toxins produced by bacteria. Memory cells remain for faster response on re-infection.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How do vaccines work?	A vaccine contains dead/weakened/inactive pathogen (or antigens). White blood cells produce antibodies and memory cells without you becoming ill. On future exposure, memory cells rapidly produce antibodies to destroy the pathogen before you get sick. Herd immunity: enough people vaccinated so the pathogen can't spread.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What is the difference between antibiotics and antivirals?	Antibiotics kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria but DO NOT work on viruses (different structure). Antivirals (e.g. antiretroviral drugs for HIV) slow viral replication but rarely cure. Antibiotic resistance develops when bacteria mutate — MRSA is a key example. Overuse of antibiotics accelerates resistance.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What are the stages of drug development and testing?	Preclinical testing: tested on cells/tissues/live animals for safety and efficacy. Clinical trials: Phase 1 — small group of healthy volunteers (safety/dosage). Phase 2 — small group of patients (effectiveness). Phase 3 — large randomised double-blind placebo-controlled trial. Peer reviewed before approval.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What plant diseases are included in the AQA spec? How are they identified and treated?	<b>Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV):</b> virus. Spread by contact. Causes discoloured mosaic pattern on leaves → reduces photosynthesis → stunted growth. No cure — use resistant varieties.<br><b>Rose black spot:</b> fungal disease. Black/purple spots on leaves → leaves turn yellow and drop. Spread by water/wind. Treated with fungicides or removing infected leaves.<br><b>Identification of disease generally:</b> observe symptoms, use lab tests (testing for pathogen's DNA/proteins), send samples for analysis.<br><b>Plant defences:</b> waxy cuticle (physical barrier), cell wall (physical barrier), chemical defences (antibacterial chemicals, poisons to deter predators). Cloze	Gonorrhoea: caused by {{c1::bacteria}}, spread by {{c2::unprotected sexual contact}}. Symptoms: {{c3::thick yellow or green discharge}} from penis or vagina, {{c4::pain when urinating}}. Treated with {{c5::antibiotics}}, but {{c6::antibiotic resistance}} is increasing — some strains now untreatable. Prevented by {{c7::condoms}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What is the required practical for culturing microorganisms? What is aseptic technique?	<b>Method:</b> sterilise agar (autoclaved). Pour into Petri dish — allow to set with lid on. Use sterile inoculating loop (flamed red-hot) to transfer bacteria onto agar. Seal with tape (not all the way round — allows some O₂ in, prevents contamination from outside). Incubate at <b>25°C in school</b> (not 37°C — prevents growth of human pathogens).<br><b>Measuring antibiotic effectiveness:</b> place antibiotic-soaked paper discs on the inoculated plate. Incubate. Measure the <b>inhibition zone</b> (clear area around disc where bacteria are killed). Larger zone = more effective antibiotic. No zone = bacteria are resistant.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What was the discovery of penicillin and why is antibiotic resistance such a concern?	<b>Discovery:</b> Alexander Fleming (1928) noticed mould (Penicillium) killed bacteria on his petri dish. Florey and Chain later developed it into a usable antibiotic (WWII).<br><b>MRSA:</b> methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — bacteria resistant to most antibiotics. Evolved through natural selection: random mutations give some bacteria resistance → antibiotics kill non-resistant bacteria → resistant bacteria survive and reproduce.<br><b>Reducing resistance:</b> complete full courses of antibiotics (don't stop when feeling better — leaves partially resistant bacteria), don't use antibiotics for viral infections, restrict use in farming. Cloze	A communicable disease is caused by a {{c1::pathogen}} — a microorganism (bacterium, virus, fungus or protist) — that can {{c2::spread from one organism to another}}. They are also called {{c3::infectious diseases}}. Transmission routes include: {{c4::direct contact}}, {{c5::droplets in the air}}, {{c6::contaminated water or food}}, and animal {{c7::vectors}} (e.g. mosquitoes). Non-communicable diseases {{c8::cannot}} spread between organisms.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Cloze	Antibodies are {{c1::proteins}} produced by {{c2::lymphocytes}} (a type of white blood cell). Each antibody has a {{c3::specific shape}} complementary to one particular {{c4::antigen}} on the pathogen's surface. Binding causes pathogens to {{c5::clump together}}, making them easier for phagocytes to engulf. After infection, {{c6::memory lymphocytes}} remain in the blood. On re-exposure to the same pathogen, they produce antibodies {{c7::much more rapidly}} — providing {{c8::immunity}}.		Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How were early medicines discovered? Give examples of drugs originally derived from natural sources.	Many medicines were originally extracted from plants or microorganisms before being chemically synthesised:<br>• <b>Aspirin</b> — pain relief and anti-inflammatory. Originally derived from willow bark (contains salicylic acid).<br>• <b>Penicillin</b> — antibiotic. Discovered by Alexander Fleming (1928) from the mould <i>Penicillium</i>.<br>• <b>Digitalis</b> — heart medicine (treats irregular heartbeat). Derived from foxglove plants.<br><b>Modern drug discovery:</b> computers and large databases are used to screen thousands of molecules for potential activity. Traditional knowledge from different cultures has guided researchers towards useful natural compounds. Once a promising compound is found, clinical trials test its safety and effectiveness.
