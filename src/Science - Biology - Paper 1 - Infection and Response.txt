Basic	What are the four types of pathogen?	Bacteria (e.g. Salmonella, gonorrhoea), Viruses (e.g. measles, HIV, tobacco mosaic virus), Fungi (e.g. rose black spot), Protists (e.g. malaria — spread by mosquito vector). Bacteria produce toxins that damage tissues. Viruses replicate inside host cells, bursting them.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response

Attacks {{c3::white blood cells}}, leading to {{c4::AIDS}} when the immune system can no longer fight infections. Controlled with {{c5::antiretroviral drugs}} — there is no cure. Basic	What are the non-specific defences of the human body?	Skin: physical barrier, produces antimicrobial secretions. Nose: hairs and mucus trap pathogens. Trachea and bronchi: ciliated cells and mucus trap and sweep out pathogens. Stomach: hydrochloric acid (pH 2) kills pathogens in food/drink.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How does the immune system respond to pathogens?	White blood cells: 1) Phagocytosis — phagocytes engulf and digest pathogens. 2) Antibody production — lymphocytes produce specific antibodies that match antigens on the pathogen, causing clumping and destruction. 3) Antitoxin production — neutralises toxins produced by bacteria. Memory cells remain for faster response on re-infection.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	How do vaccines work?	A vaccine contains dead/weakened/inactive pathogen (or antigens). White blood cells produce antibodies and memory cells without you becoming ill. On future exposure, memory cells rapidly produce antibodies to destroy the pathogen before you get sick. Herd immunity: enough people vaccinated so the pathogen can't spread.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What is the difference between antibiotics and antivirals?	Antibiotics kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria but DO NOT work on viruses (different structure). Antivirals (e.g. antiretroviral drugs for HIV) slow viral replication but rarely cure. Antibiotic resistance develops when bacteria mutate — MRSA is a key example. Overuse of antibiotics accelerates resistance.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What are the stages of drug development and testing?	Preclinical testing: tested on cells/tissues/live animals for safety and efficacy. Clinical trials: Phase 1 — small group of healthy volunteers (safety/dosage). Phase 2 — small group of patients (effectiveness). Phase 3 — large randomised double-blind placebo-controlled trial. Peer reviewed before approval.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What plant diseases are included in the AQA spec? How are they identified and treated?	<b>Tobacco mosaic virus (TMV):</b> virus. Spread by contact. Causes discoloured mosaic pattern on leaves → reduces photosynthesis → stunted growth. No cure — use resistant varieties.<br><b>Rose black spot:</b> fungal disease. Black/purple spots on leaves → leaves turn yellow and drop. Spread by water/wind. Treated with fungicides or removing infected leaves.<br><b>Identification of disease generally:</b> observe symptoms, use lab tests (testing for pathogen's DNA/proteins), send samples for analysis.<br><b>Plant defences:</b> waxy cuticle (physical barrier), cell wall (physical barrier), chemical defences (antibacterial chemicals, poisons to deter predators). Basic	What is the required practical for culturing microorganisms? What is aseptic technique?	<b>Method:</b> sterilise agar (autoclaved). Pour into Petri dish — allow to set with lid on. Use sterile inoculating loop (flamed red-hot) to transfer bacteria onto agar. Seal with tape (not all the way round — allows some O₂ in, prevents contamination from outside). Incubate at <b>25°C in school</b> (not 37°C — prevents growth of human pathogens).<br><b>Measuring antibiotic effectiveness:</b> place antibiotic-soaked paper discs on the inoculated plate. Incubate. Measure the <b>inhibition zone</b> (clear area around disc where bacteria are killed). Larger zone = more effective antibiotic. No zone = bacteria are resistant.	Science::Biology::Paper 1::Infection and Response
Basic	What was the discovery of penicillin and why is antibiotic resistance such a concern?	<b>Discovery:</b> Alexander Fleming (1928) noticed mould (Penicillium) killed bacteria on his petri dish. Florey and Chain later developed it into a usable antibiotic (WWII).<br><b>MRSA:</b> methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus — bacteria resistant to most antibiotics. Evolved through natural selection: random mutations give some bacteria resistance → antibiotics kill non-resistant bacteria → resistant bacteria survive and reproduce.<br><b>Reducing resistance:</b> complete full courses of antibiotics (don't stop when feeling better — leaves partially resistant bacteria), don't use antibiotics for viral infections, restrict use in farming.
