Basic	What is the difference between transverse and longitudinal waves?	Transverse: oscillations perpendicular to direction of travel (e.g. light, water waves, electromagnetic waves). Longitudinal: oscillations parallel to direction of travel (e.g. sound, P-waves). Longitudinal waves have compressions (high pressure) and rarefactions (low pressure).	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the electromagnetic spectrum and its uses?	Radio waves: TV/radio communication. Microwaves: satellite communication, cooking. Infrared: thermal imaging, TV remotes, optical fibres. Visible light: sight, photography. UV: fluorescent lamps, sterilisation, detecting forged bank notes. X-rays: medical imaging, security scanning. Gamma: cancer treatment, sterilising medical equipment. All travel at 3×10⁸ m/s in a vacuum.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Cloze	Refraction: waves change {{c1::speed}} (and direction if hitting at an angle) when passing between media. Light slows down in {{c2::denser}} materials — bends {{c3::towards the normal}}. Used in {{c4::lenses}} and optical fibres.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	How are sound waves produced and what affects their properties?	Sound: longitudinal wave produced by vibrating objects. Travels through solids, liquids and gases (not vacuum). Frequency determines pitch (higher f = higher pitch). Amplitude determines loudness. Speed in air ~330 m/s. Ultrasound (>20,000 Hz) used in medical imaging and sonar.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Cloze	The EM spectrum in order of increasing frequency (lowest to highest): {{c1::radio waves}}, {{c2::microwaves}}, {{c3::infrared}}, {{c4::visible light}}, {{c5::ultraviolet}}, {{c6::X-rays}}, {{c7::gamma rays}}. All travel at {{c8::3×10⁸ m/s}} in a vacuum.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Cloze	Going from radio to gamma: frequency {{c1::increases}}, wavelength {{c2::decreases}}, energy {{c3::increases}}. Higher frequency = more {{c4::ionising}} and more {{c5::dangerous}} to living tissue.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of radio waves?	Longest wavelength, lowest frequency and energy. Produced by oscillating electrons. Uses: TV and radio broadcasting, Bluetooth, WiFi. Hazards: generally considered safe at normal exposure levels — non-ionising.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of microwaves?	Between radio and infrared. Absorbed by water molecules — causes heating. Uses: cooking (microwave ovens), satellite communication, mobile phones, radar. Hazards: can heat body tissue internally — non-ionising but can cause heating of cells.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of infrared radiation?	Just below visible light. All objects emit infrared — hotter objects emit more. Detected by skin as heat. Uses: thermal imaging cameras, TV remotes, optical fibre communication, night vision, toasters/grills, physiotherapy. Hazards: can cause skin burns and eye damage at high intensities.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of visible light?	The only part of the EM spectrum detectable by the human eye. Range from red (lowest frequency) to violet (highest). Uses: sight, photography, optical fibres for communication, lasers (surgery, barcode scanners). Hazards: high intensity lasers or bright light can damage the retina.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of ultraviolet (UV) radiation?	Higher frequency than visible light — ionising. Produced by the Sun and UV lamps. Uses: detecting forged bank notes (security markings fluoresce), sterilising water/medical equipment, tanning beds. Hazards: damages DNA → skin cancer, damages eyes → cataracts. Ozone layer absorbs most UV from the Sun.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of X-rays?	High frequency, short wavelength — ionising. Produced by firing electrons at metal targets. Absorbed by dense materials (bone/metal), pass through soft tissue. Uses: medical imaging (bone fractures, dental checks), security scanning (airports), checking welds in industry. Hazards: ionises cells → can cause mutations and cancer. Radiographers move away or use lead screens.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What are the properties, uses and hazards of gamma rays?	Highest frequency, shortest wavelength — most ionising. Emitted by unstable atomic nuclei (radioactive decay). Uses: cancer treatment (radiotherapy — kills tumour cells), sterilising medical equipment, food irradiation (kills bacteria), PET scans. Hazards: highly ionising — damages/kills cells and DNA → cancer. Reduced by thick lead or concrete.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Cloze	Non-ionising radiation ({{c1::radio, microwave, infrared, visible}}) does not have enough energy to remove electrons from atoms. Ionising radiation ({{c2::UV, X-rays, gamma}}) has enough energy to ionise atoms, potentially causing {{c3::cancer}} by damaging {{c4::DNA}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What is the difference between reflection, absorption and transmission of EM waves?	Reflection: wave bounces off a surface (angle of incidence = angle of reflection). Absorption: wave transfers energy to the material — causes heating. Transmission: wave passes through the material. Different materials reflect, absorb and transmit different parts of the EM spectrum — e.g. glass transmits visible light but absorbs UV.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Cloze	Amplitude = {{c1::maximum displacement}} from rest position. Frequency = number of {{c2::complete waves per second}} (Hz). Wavelength = distance from one {{c3::crest to the next crest}} (or trough to trough). Period = {{c4::1 ÷ frequency}} (time for one complete wave).		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
Basic	What is the required practical for waves? How do you measure the speed of waves?	<b>Ripple tank (water waves):</b> measure wavelength (distance between wave crests on stroboscope or freeze-frame). Count frequency (oscillations per second). Speed = fλ.<br><b>Sound waves in a solid (slinky):</b> send a wave pulse, measure time for it to travel known distance. Speed = distance/time.<br><b>Measuring speed of sound in air:</b> stand far from a wall. Clap boards together and time the echo (echo = sound travels twice the distance). Speed ≈ 330 m/s. Or use two microphones connected to an oscilloscope — measure time delay for sound to travel between them.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Waves
