Basic	What is crude oil and how is it separated?	Crude oil is a mixture of hydrocarbons (compounds of C and H only). Separated by fractional distillation — heated, vapours rise through a fractionating column, condense at different temperatures (different boiling points). Shorter chain = lower boiling point = more flammable = less viscous.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Cloze	Alkanes: general formula {{c1::CₙH₂ₙ₊₂}}. Saturated hydrocarbons — only {{c2::single}} C-C bonds. First four: {{c3::methane (CH₄)}}, {{c4::ethane (C₂H₆)}}, {{c5::propane (C₃H₈)}}, {{c6::butane (C₄H₁₀)}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Cloze	Alkenes: general formula {{c1::CₙH₂ₙ}}. Unsaturated — contain a {{c2::C=C double bond}}. Test with {{c3::bromine water}}: alkenes turn it from {{c4::orange/brown to colourless}} (decolourises). Alkanes do NOT decolourise bromine water.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Basic	What is cracking and why is it important?	Long-chain alkanes are cracked (broken down) to produce shorter, more useful molecules — alkanes and alkenes. Two methods: catalytic cracking (zeolite catalyst, ~500°C) and steam cracking (steam, very high temperature). Important because demand for short-chain hydrocarbons (fuels, plastics) exceeds supply from fractional distillation.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Basic	What are the products of complete vs incomplete combustion?	Complete combustion (excess O₂): hydrocarbon + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O. Incomplete combustion (limited O₂): produces CO (toxic, colourless, odourless) + C (soot/particulates) + H₂O. Soot causes global dimming and respiratory problems. CO is toxic — binds to haemoglobin preventing O₂ transport.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Basic	What are the addition reactions of alkenes?	Alkenes react across the C=C double bond with various reagents — one product formed:<br><b>+ H₂ (hydrogenation):</b> alkene + hydrogen → alkane (Ni catalyst, ~150°C). E.g. ethene + H₂ → ethane. Used to make margarine from vegetable oils.<br><b>+ Br₂ (bromination):</b> alkene + bromine → dibromoalkane. Bromine water decolourises (orange → colourless). Test for C=C bond.<br><b>+ H₂O (hydration):</b> alkene + steam → alcohol (H₃PO₄ catalyst, ~300°C). E.g. ethene + H₂O → ethanol. Industrial method for ethanol. Cloze	Alcohols general formula: {{c1::CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH}}. Ethanol: {{c2::C₂H₅OH}}. Made by <b>fermentation</b>: glucose + yeast → {{c3::ethanol}} + {{c4::CO₂}} (anaerobic, ~30°C, pH 7). OR by <b>hydration</b> of ethene: ethene + steam → ethanol ({{c5::H₃PO₄ catalyst, ~300°C}}). Fermentation = renewable but slow; hydration = non-renewable but faster and continuous. Cloze	Carboxylic acids have the functional group {{c1::–COOH}}. Ethanoic acid = {{c2::CH₃COOH}} (vinegar). They are {{c3::weak}} acids (partially ionise in water). React with alcohols to form {{c4::esters}} + water — called {{c5::esterification}}. E.g. ethanoic acid + ethanol ⇌ ethyl ethanoate + water (catalyst: {{c6::concentrated H₂SO₄}}). Basic	What is addition polymerisation and how do you represent the polymer from a monomer?	Many alkene monomers (with C=C double bonds) join together — the double bond opens and molecules link into a long chain. Only <b>one product</b> forms — atom economy = 100%.<br><b>Example:</b> n(CH₂=CH₂) → [–CH₂–CH₂–]ₙ (polyethene/polythene)<br>n(CH₂=CHCl) → [–CH₂–CHCl–]ₙ (PVC)<br><b>Representing:</b> the monomer shows C=C; the repeat unit in polymer shows single bond. Brackets and subscript n show the repeating unit.<br><b>Environmental problem:</b> most addition polymers are non-biodegradable.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Organic Chemistry
Basic	What are condensation polymers? Give examples of nylon and polyester.	Formed by joining monomers together with elimination of a small molecule (usually water or HCl) with each bond formed.<br><b>Requires two functional groups on each monomer</b> (bifunctional).<br><b>Polyester:</b> diol + dicarboxylic acid → polyester + water. E.g. Terylene from ethane-1,2-diol + benzene-1,4-dicarboxylic acid.<br><b>Nylon:</b> diamine + dicarboxylic acid (or diacid chloride) → nylon + water. Used in ropes, clothing, toothbrush bristles.<br><b>Difference from addition polymers:</b> condensation polymers contain heteroatoms (O or N) in the backbone; addition polymers are all carbon chains.
