Basic	What are the properties of solids, liquids and gases in terms of particle theory?	Solid: particles close together in fixed positions, vibrate — fixed shape and volume. Liquid: particles close together but can move — fixed volume, no fixed shape. Gas: particles far apart, move randomly and fast — no fixed shape or volume. As energy increases, particles move faster. Basic	What is gas pressure and how does it change with temperature?	Gas pressure caused by particles colliding with walls of container. Increasing temperature → particles move faster → more frequent and forceful collisions → increased pressure (if volume constant). Increasing volume → fewer collisions per unit area → decreased pressure (if temperature constant).	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Particle Model of Matter

Basic	What is the required practical for measuring density? How do you find the volume of irregular objects?	<b>Regular objects:</b> measure dimensions with ruler/vernier callipers → calculate volume (e.g. l×w×h for cuboid). Measure mass on digital balance → density = mass/volume.<br><b>Irregular objects (e.g. stone):</b> partially fill a measuring cylinder with water, note the volume. Submerge the object → note new volume. Volume of object = difference in water levels (displacement method).<br>Measure mass on balance → density = mass/volume.<br><b>Units:</b> if mass in g and volume in cm³ → density in g/cm³. Basic	Worked example: SHC calculation. A 2 kg block of copper (SHC = 385 J/kg°C) is heated and its temperature rises from 20°C to 70°C. How much energy was transferred?	Q = mcΔT<br>m = 2 kg, c = 385 J/kg°C, ΔT = 70 − 20 = 50°C<br>Q = 2 × 385 × 50 = <b>38,500 J = 38.5 kJ</b><br><b>Rearranging:</b> c = Q/(mΔT) to find SHC. m = Q/(cΔT) to find mass. ΔT = Q/(mc) to find temperature change.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Particle Model of Matter
Basic	What is the required practical for specific heat capacity? What is the method?	<b>Equipment:</b> metal block (aluminium or copper) with two holes, immersion heater, thermometer, joulemeter or ammeter+voltmeter+stopwatch, insulating material.<br><b>Method:</b> insert heater and thermometer into block. Record starting temperature. Switch on heater, record temperature every minute and the total energy supplied (from joulemeter: E = IVt).<br><b>Calculate:</b> c = Q ÷ (mΔT) where Q = energy from joulemeter, m = mass of block, ΔT = temperature rise.<br><b>Wrap block in insulating material</b> to reduce energy lost to surroundings → more accurate result. Basic	Worked example: specific latent heat. How much energy is needed to melt 0.5 kg of ice? (Latent heat of fusion of water = 334,000 J/kg)	Q = mL<br>Q = 0.5 × 334,000 = <b>167,000 J = 167 kJ</b><br><b>Key point:</b> during melting, temperature stays at 0°C — all energy breaks intermolecular bonds, none goes into kinetic energy of particles.<br><b>Latent heat of vaporisation</b> (boiling water → steam) = 2,260,000 J/kg — much larger because bonds between molecules must be completely separated. Basic	(Higher Tier) What is the pressure-temperature law for gases and how do you use it?	At constant volume: p/T = constant, so <b>p₁/T₁ = p₂/T₂</b>.<br><b>Important:</b> temperature MUST be in Kelvin (K). T(K) = T(°C) + 273.<br><b>Example:</b> gas at 100 kPa and 27°C (= 300 K). Heated to 127°C (= 400 K) at constant volume.<br>p₂ = p₁ × T₂/T₁ = 100 × 400/300 = <b>133 kPa</b><br><b>Explanation:</b> higher temperature → faster particles → more frequent/harder collisions with walls → greater pressure. Basic	What is Brownian motion and what does it show about particles?	<b>Brownian motion:</b> the random, jittery movement of small particles (e.g. smoke or pollen) suspended in a fluid, observed under a microscope.<br><b>Explanation:</b> the particles are constantly being bombarded by much smaller, invisible molecules of the fluid moving randomly. The bombardment is uneven — more molecules hit from one side at any moment → net force → random movement.<br><b>Significance:</b> provides evidence that fluids are made of tiny, constantly moving particles (kinetic theory). First explained by Einstein in 1905.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Particle Model of Matter
