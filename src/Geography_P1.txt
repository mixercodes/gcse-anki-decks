Basic	What is hydraulic action (coastal erosion) and how does it erode cliffs?	The force of waves compresses air into cracks in the cliff face. The pressure expands the crack until rock breaks off.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	What is abrasion (coastal erosion) and how does it erode cliffs?	Rocks and sediment picked up by waves are hurled against the cliff face, wearing it away. Also called corrasion.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	What is attrition (coastal erosion) and what does it produce?	Rocks and pebbles in the water collide with each other, breaking into smaller, rounder, smoother pieces. Produces sand and shingle.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	What is solution (coastal erosion) and which rock types does it affect?	Acidic seawater chemically dissolves soluble rock such as chalk and limestone. Also called corrosion.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	What is longshore drift and how does it work?	Waves approach at an angle (driven by prevailing wind). Swash carries sediment up the beach at that angle. Backwash pulls sediment straight back down due to gravity. Net movement of sediment along the coast.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	How is a spit formed?	Longshore drift carries sediment along the coast. Where the coastline changes direction (e.g. at a river mouth) sediment is deposited and extends out to sea. Secondary winds curve the end. A lagoon may form behind the spit.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	How is a wave-cut platform formed?	Waves attack the base of a cliff through hydraulic action and abrasion, forming a wave-cut notch. The cliff above becomes unsupported and collapses. Repeated over time, the cliff retreats leaving a flat rocky platform at its base.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts
Basic	What is the difference between hard and soft coastal engineering?	Hard: artificial structures (sea walls, groynes, rock armour) — expensive, effective short-term, can disrupt sediment transfer. Soft: works with natural processes (beach nourishment, dune regeneration, managed retreat) — cheaper and more sustainable.	Geography::Paper 1::Coasts

Basic	How does a river change from upper to lower course?	Upper: narrow, shallow, fast, V-shaped valleys, vertical erosion, interlocking spurs, waterfalls. Basic	How is a waterfall formed?	Hard rock overlies softer rock. Softer rock erodes faster undercutting the hard rock. A plunge pool forms from hydraulic action. The overhang collapses. The waterfall retreats upstream forming a gorge.	Geography::Paper 1::Rivers
Basic	How are meanders and ox-bow lakes formed?	Faster water on the outside of a bend causes erosion (river cliff). Slower water on inside deposits sediment (slip-off slope). Bend becomes more exaggerated. River breaks through during flood, cutting off the meander — ox-bow lake forms.	Geography::Paper 1::Rivers
Basic	How are river levees formed?	During floods the river overflows and slows, depositing heaviest sediment closest to the channel. Over time deposits build up, raising the river bank above the surrounding floodplain.	Geography::Paper 1::Rivers
Basic	What is the difference between hard and soft flood management?	Hard: dams/reservoirs, channel straightening, embankments/levees, flood relief channels. Soft: afforestation (slows runoff), floodplain zoning (no building in high-risk areas), flood warnings, managed flooding of fields.	Geography::Paper 1::Rivers
Basic	How is a corrie formed?	Snow collects in a hollow on a north-facing slope and compresses into ice. Ice moves rotationally, eroding through abrasion and plucking. A rock lip forms at the front. When glacier melts a tarn (lake) may remain.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	How does freeze-thaw weathering occur?	Water enters cracks in rock. Freezes and expands by 9%, widening the crack. Thaws and refreezes repeatedly. Eventually the rock shatters producing angular scree at the base of slopes.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is a U-shaped valley (glacial landform) and how does it form?	A glacier erodes a V-shaped river valley downward and outward — widening and deepening it into a U-shape. Leaves steep sides and a flat floor after ice melts.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is a corrie (cirque) and how does it form?	A circular hollow in a mountainside. Snow collects and compresses into ice, eroding the hollow by plucking and abrasion. A rock lip forms at the front; a tarn may form when the ice melts.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is an arête and how does it form?	A knife-edge ridge formed when two corries erode back-to-back on either side of a mountain, leaving a narrow wall of rock between them.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is a pyramidal peak and how does it form?	A sharp mountain summit formed when three or more corries erode into the same mountain from different directions. Example: the Matterhorn.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is a hanging valley and what does it create?	A tributary valley left high above the main glacier valley floor after the main glacier erodes much deeper. Often has a waterfall at its junction with the main valley.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation
Basic	What is a ribbon lake and how does it form?	A long narrow lake in a U-shaped valley, formed in an overdeepened rock basin eroded by a glacier where rock was softer. Often dammed by glacial deposits at the valley end.	Geography::Paper 1::Glaciation

Basic	What is an ecosystem and how do its components interact?	A community of living organisms (biotic: plants, animals, decomposers) interacting with non-living elements (abiotic: soil, water, temperature, light). Energy flows through food chains from producers to consumers. Nutrients are recycled by decomposers. Change to one component ripples through the whole system.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Ecosystems
Basic	What is a food chain and what do the arrows represent?	A sequence showing how energy passes from one organism to another. Arrow direction = direction of energy transfer (e.g. grass → rabbit → fox). Producers (plants) form the base. Primary consumers eat producers. Secondary consumers eat primary consumers. Energy is lost at each stage as heat and waste.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Ecosystems
Basic	What is the nutrient cycle and how does it work?	Nutrients are taken up from the soil by producers. Passed through consumers in the food chain. Returned to soil by decomposers breaking down dead organic matter. Key inputs: rainfall (leaches nutrients in), weathering. Key outputs: runoff, burning. The cycle is not perfect — deforestation breaks it.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Ecosystems
Basic	What is the difference between biotic and abiotic components?	Biotic: living parts of an ecosystem — producers (plants), consumers (animals), decomposers (fungi, bacteria). Abiotic: non-living — temperature, rainfall, soil type, light levels, aspect. Both must be balanced for an ecosystem to function. Changing one component affects all others.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Ecosystems
Basic	What are the global biomes and their distribution?	Tropical rainforest (5°N–5°S, high rainfall year-round). Savanna (tropical grassland, seasonal rainfall). Hot desert (20-30° N/S, less than 250mm/yr rain). Temperate grassland. Deciduous/temperate forest. Boreal (taiga) forest. Tundra (Arctic fringe, permafrost). Distribution determined by temperature and rainfall (climate).	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Ecosystems
Basic	What is the emergent layer of a tropical rainforest?	The tallest trees (40–60m) with isolated, umbrella-shaped crowns. Receive the most sunlight but face the most wind and temperature variation.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	What is the canopy layer of a tropical rainforest?	A dense continuous layer of overlapping tree crowns (25–40m). Blocks up to 80% of sunlight reaching lower layers. Contains the greatest biodiversity of animal life.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	What is the understorey layer of a tropical rainforest?	Shrubs and small trees (10–25m) adapted to shade. Plants have large, dark leaves to capture limited light. Includes young trees waiting for a gap in the canopy above.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	What is the forest floor layer of a tropical rainforest?	Very dark — little light penetrates here. Sparse vegetation. Dead matter decomposes rapidly in the heat and humidity, returning nutrients to the soil almost immediately.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	What are buttress roots and why do tropical rainforest trees have them?	Large triangular root extensions that spread from the base of tall trees. They provide structural support in shallow, nutrient-poor soils where deep roots cannot anchor the tree.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	Why do tropical rainforest plant leaves have drip tips?	Drip tips (pointed leaf ends) allow excess rainfall to run off quickly, preventing algae and moss from growing on the leaf surface and blocking sunlight.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	What is an epiphyte and why do they exist in the tropical rainforest?	A plant that grows on another plant (e.g. a tree) to reach the light in the canopy, without rooting in the soil. Examples: orchids, bromeliads. They are not parasitic.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	Why do tropical rainforest trees have shallow, wide root systems?	Nutrients exist only in the top few centimetres of soil. Shallow roots spread widely to capture nutrients before heavy rainfall leaches them out of reach.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests
Basic	Why are TRF soils nutrient-poor despite lush vegetation?	Nutrients are stored mainly in biomass, not soil. Heavy rainfall leaches nutrients out of the soil quickly. The nutrient cycle is rapid — decomposers break down litter fast in the heat/humidity, releasing nutrients directly back to shallow roots. When trees are cleared, the cycle breaks and soils are exposed to leaching and erosion.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Rainforests

Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Cold Environments
Basic	What is permafrost and why does it limit biodiversity?	Permanently frozen ground below the surface (active layer melts briefly in summer). Only 20-30cm of topsoil thaws. Tree roots cannot penetrate the frozen layer. Waterlogging occurs when melt cannot drain. Only low-growing, shallow-rooted plants survive (mosses, lichens, sedges, cotton grass). Very few species → fragile ecosystem.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Cold Environments
Basic	How do animals adapt to cold environments?	Thick layers of fat (insulation). Dense fur/feathers — white colouring for camouflage in snow. Small ears and limbs (reduce heat loss — Allen's Rule). Hibernation to survive winter food shortages. Seasonal migration to warmer areas. Examples: Arctic fox, polar bear, caribou, snowy owl, lemmings.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Cold Environments
Basic	How do plants adapt to cold environments?	Grow low and compact to stay below the snow line (sheltered from wind). Small, waxy or hairy leaves to reduce water loss. Dark colours to absorb maximum solar energy. Shallow, wide root systems (can't penetrate permafrost). Rapid growth/reproduction in short summer. Examples: mosses, lichens, Arctic willow, cotton grass.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Cold Environments
Basic	Why are cold environments under threat from development and how should they be managed?	Threats: oil/mineral extraction (oil spills, habitat damage, roads disrupt migration). Tourism footprint. Global warming melting permafrost. Management: international agreements (Antarctic Treaty, whaling ban). National laws (US National Petroleum Reserve). Conservation groups. Technology (directional drilling, elevated pipelines). Balancing development vs preservation.	Geography::Paper 1::Living World - Cold Environments
Basic	What are the physical causes of flooding?	Heavy prolonged rainfall saturates the soil (increased surface runoff). Impermeable rock speeds runoff. Steep slopes (short lag time). Snowmelt. Lag time = the delay between peak rainfall and peak discharge. Short lag time = flood risk high (steep slopes, urban, deforested). Long lag time = water absorbed slowly (vegetation, permeable rock).
