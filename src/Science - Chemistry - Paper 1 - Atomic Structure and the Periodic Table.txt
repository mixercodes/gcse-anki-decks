Basic	How is the periodic table organised?	Elements arranged in order of atomic number. Vertical columns = groups (elements with same number of outer electrons, similar properties). Horizontal rows = periods (elements with same number of electron shells). Metals on left, non-metals on right, separated by a zigzag line.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table
Basic	What are the properties of Group 1 (alkali metals) and how do they change down the group?	React with water to produce hydrogen gas and a metal hydroxide (alkaline solution). React with oxygen to form metal oxides. Going down Group 1: increased reactivity (outer electron further from nucleus, easier to lose), lower melting/boiling points, less dense.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table
Basic	What are the properties of Group 7 (halogens)?	Non-metals that exist as diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂). Going down: less reactive (harder to gain an electron), higher melting/boiling points, darker colour (F=pale yellow, Cl=green, Br=orange/brown, I=grey solid/purple vapour). More reactive halogens displace less reactive ones from solutions.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table
Basic	What are the properties of Group 0 (noble gases)?	Inert (unreactive) because they have full outer electron shells. Monatomic (exist as single atoms). Going down: higher boiling points (stronger intermolecular forces). Used in filament bulbs (Ar), balloons (He), discharge lighting (Ne/Ar).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table

Basic	What are the trends down Group 1 (alkali metals) in reactivity, melting point and appearance?	<b>Going down Group 1 (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs):</b><br>• <b>Reactivity increases:</b> outer electron is in a higher shell, further from nucleus, less strongly attracted → easier to lose → more reactive. Shielding from inner electrons also increases.<br>• <b>Melting and boiling points decrease</b> (unusual — softer metals)<br>• <b>Density increases</b><br>• <b>React with water:</b> Li — fizzes gently; Na — fizzes vigorously, may catch fire; K — catches fire immediately with lilac flame.<br><b>All reactions with water:</b> metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen. E.g. 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Atomic Structure and the Periodic Table
Basic	What are the trends down Group 7 (halogens) in reactivity and physical state?	<b>Going down Group 7 (F, Cl, Br, I):</b><br>• <b>Reactivity decreases:</b> gaining an electron becomes harder as the outer shell is further from the nucleus and there is more shielding. Less electronegativity.<br>• <b>Physical state at room temperature:</b> F₂ = pale yellow gas; Cl₂ = green gas; Br₂ = orange/brown liquid; I₂ = grey solid (purple vapour)<br>• <b>Melting/boiling points increase</b> (stronger intermolecular forces)<br><b>Displacement:</b> Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂ (Cl₂ displaces Br⁻ because Cl is more reactive — solution turns orange-brown). Basic	What are the transition metals and what properties do they share?	The transition metals are found in the <b>central block (periods 4–7, groups 3–12)</b> of the periodic table — e.g. iron, copper, nickel, chromium, titanium.<br><b>Properties:</b> high melting and boiling points (except mercury — liquid at room temperature). High density. Good conductors of heat and electricity. Hard and strong (except Hg). Form <b>coloured compounds</b> (e.g. CuSO₄ = blue, FeCl₃ = orange). Can act as <b>catalysts</b> (iron in Haber process, nickel in hydrogenation). Often have multiple <b>oxidation states</b> (e.g. Fe²⁺ and Fe³⁺).
