Basic	What is the difference between scalar and vector quantities?	Scalar: magnitude only (e.g. speed, distance, mass, temperature, time). Vector: magnitude AND direction (e.g. velocity, displacement, acceleration, force, momentum, weight). Vectors are represented by arrows.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces

Basic	What do distance-time and velocity-time graphs show?	Distance-time: gradient = speed. Horizontal line = stationary. Curved line = acceleration. Velocity-time: gradient = acceleration. Area under graph = distance. Horizontal line = constant velocity. Negative gradient = deceleration.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Cloze	Newton's Second Law: {{c1::F = ma}}. Force in {{c2::Newtons (N)}}, mass in {{c3::kg}}, acceleration in {{c4::m/s²}}. A 1000kg car accelerating at 2m/s²: F = {{c5::2000N}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Basic	What are the three laws of motion?	1st: An object remains at rest or moves with constant velocity unless acted on by a resultant force. 2nd: F = ma (resultant force = mass × acceleration). 3rd: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction — forces act on different objects.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces

Braking distance affected by: {{c4::speed, road conditions, tyre condition, brake condition}}. Braking force causes {{c5::deceleration}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces

Basic	What is the principle of moments?	For a system in equilibrium (not rotating): sum of clockwise moments = sum of anticlockwise moments. Moment = force × perpendicular distance from pivot. Units: Newton-metres (Nm).	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Cloze	Momentum = {{c1::mass}} × {{c2::velocity}}. Units: {{c3::kg m/s}}. Law of conservation of momentum: total momentum before = total momentum after in a {{c4::closed system}} (no external forces). Force = {{c5::change in momentum}} ÷ time.		Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Basic	What is Hooke's Law and what is the spring constant?	<b>Hooke's Law:</b> extension of a spring is directly proportional to the force applied, up to the limit of proportionality (elastic limit).<br><b>Formula:</b> F = ke (Force = spring constant × extension). k in N/m, e in m.<br><b>Graph:</b> linear section (Hooke's Law applies) then curves upward past the elastic limit (plastic deformation — doesn't return to original shape).<br><b>Worked example:</b> 5N stretches a spring by 0.1m → k = F/e = 5/0.1 = 50 N/m. Predict extension for 7.5N: e = F/k = 7.5/50 = 0.15 m.<br><b>Required practical:</b> measure extension for increasing weights, plot F vs extension.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Basic	What is pressure and how do you calculate it? What is pressure in a fluid?	<b>Pressure = force ÷ area</b> (P = F/A). Units: Pascals (Pa) or N/m².<br><b>Example:</b> 500N on area of 0.25m² → P = 500/0.25 = 2000 Pa.<br><b>Pressure in a fluid</b> increases with depth: P = hρg (h = depth, ρ = density, g = 9.8 N/kg).<br><b>Atmospheric pressure</b> decreases with altitude (less air above → less weight → less pressure).<br><b>Upthrust:</b> pressure in a fluid is greater at the bottom than top of an object → net upward force = upthrust. If upthrust ≥ weight, object floats.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Basic	What is the difference between elastic and inelastic collisions?	<b>Elastic collision:</b> both momentum AND kinetic energy are conserved. Ideal — KE not lost to heat/deformation. Example: billiard balls (approximately elastic).<br><b>Inelastic collision:</b> momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is NOT (some converted to heat, sound, deformation). Most real collisions. Example: car crash — objects crumple (designed — increases collision time → reduces force → safer).<br><b>In all collisions:</b> momentum is always conserved (closed system). Total KE after ≤ total KE before.	Science::Physics::Paper 2::Forces
Basic	What are terminal velocity and the forces on falling objects?	<b>Falling object:</b> initially, weight > drag → accelerates. As speed increases, drag increases. Eventually drag = weight → resultant force = 0 → constant velocity = terminal velocity.<br><b>Graph (velocity vs time):</b> curve increasing, then horizontal line at terminal velocity.<br><b>Parachute example:</b> when parachute opens → drag suddenly increases greatly → deceleration. New, lower terminal velocity reached. On landing, speed is safe.<br><b>Factors affecting terminal velocity:</b> weight (greater weight → higher terminal velocity), drag (larger area/higher drag coefficient → lower terminal velocity).
