Basic	How do you test for hydrogen, oxygen, CO₂ and chlorine gas?	Hydrogen: burning splint → squeaky pop. Oxygen: glowing splint → relights. CO₂: limewater → turns milky/cloudy. Chlorine: damp litmus paper → bleaches it white.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 2::Chemical Analysis
Basic	What is paper chromatography used for and how do you calculate Rf?	Identifies substances in a mixture by how far they travel up chromatography paper. Rf = distance travelled by substance ÷ distance travelled by solvent. Pure substances give one spot. Can be used to identify unknown substances by comparing Rf values. Basic	What is a pure substance and what is a formulation?	Pure substance: only one type of element or compound — has sharp melting/boiling point. Formulation: deliberately designed mixture with specific proportions for a purpose (e.g. medicines, fuels, paints, fertilisers, foods). Impure substances melt/boil over a range of temperatures. Basic	How do you test for carbonate ions (CO₃²⁻)?	Add dilute acid (e.g. dilute HCl) to the solid or solution. If carbonate ions are present, <b>fizzing occurs</b> and carbon dioxide gas is released.<br>CO₃²⁻ + 2H⁺ → CO₂ + H₂O<br>Confirm the gas is CO₂ by passing through limewater — turns <b>milky/cloudy</b>. Basic	How do you test for sulfate ions (SO₄²⁻)?	Add dilute hydrochloric acid (to remove interfering ions such as carbonates) then add <b>barium chloride solution (BaCl₂)</b>.<br>If sulfate ions are present: a <b>white precipitate</b> of barium sulfate (BaSO₄) forms — insoluble in acid.<br>Ba²⁺ + SO₄²⁻ → BaSO₄ (white precipitate)<br>The HCl ensures any carbonate precipitate (which is also white) would dissolve, confirming the result is truly from sulfate. Basic	How do you test for ammonium ions (NH₄⁺)?	Add sodium hydroxide solution (NaOH) and <b>gently warm</b>. If ammonium ions are present, <b>ammonia gas (NH₄⁺ + OH⁻ → NH₃ + H₂O)</b> is released — has a distinctive pungent smell.<br>Confirm with <b>damp red litmus paper</b> → turns <b>blue</b> (ammonia is alkaline). Basic	What is the required practical for chromatography? What are the key technique points?	<b>Method:</b> draw a <b>pencil</b> baseline (not pen — ink dissolves) on chromatography paper. Spot samples onto the line using a capillary tube. Allow spots to dry. Stand paper in solvent <b>below the baseline</b>. Allow solvent to rise. Remove before solvent reaches top. Mark <b>solvent front</b> immediately.<br><b>Calculate Rf</b> = distance moved by spot ÷ distance moved by solvent front.<br><b>Key points:</b> use pencil not pen; keep solvent below baseline; don't move paper while running; cover to prevent evaporation. Basic	What is instrumental analysis and what are its advantages over chemical tests?	<b>Flame emission spectroscopy:</b> elements identified by the specific wavelengths of light they emit when heated. Produces a unique line spectrum for each element. Used to identify metal ions in solution.<br><b>Mass spectrometry:</b> identifies substances by their mass-to-charge ratio. Can identify very small amounts of unknown substances.<br><b>Advantages over chemical tests:</b> more sensitive (detect tiny quantities), more accurate, faster results, can identify substances that give similar chemical test results. Basic	How would you identify an unknown ionic compound using a systematic sequence of tests?	<b>Step 1:</b> Flame test → identifies metal cation (Na = yellow, K = lilac, Ca = orange-red, Cu = green/blue, Li = crimson).<br><b>Step 2:</b> Add NaOH → precipitate colour identifies other metal ions (Cu²⁺ blue, Fe²⁺ green, Fe³⁺ brown, Al³⁺ white dissolves in excess).<br><b>Step 3:</b> Add dilute acid → fizzes = carbonate (CO₃²⁻) present; test gas with limewater.<br><b>Step 4:</b> Add AgNO₃ after HNO₃ → white/cream/yellow ppt = Cl⁻/Br⁻/I⁻.<br><b>Step 5:</b> Add BaCl₂ after HCl → white ppt = sulfate (SO₄²⁻). Basic	An unknown compound gives a yellow flame test. Adding NaOH gives no precipitate. Adding AgNO₃ (after HNO₃) gives a cream precipitate. Identify the compound.	<b>Yellow flame</b> → sodium (Na⁺) ion present.<br><b>No precipitate with NaOH</b> → no copper, iron or aluminium ions (confirms Na⁺).<br><b>Cream precipitate with AgNO₃</b> → bromide (Br⁻) ion present (AgBr is cream).<br><b>Compound = sodium bromide (NaBr)</b>.<br><b>Exam tip:</b> always name BOTH the cation AND anion to fully identify an ionic compound.
