Basic	What is longshore drift and how do groynes affect it?	<b>Longshore drift:</b> waves approach the shore at an angle (the angle of prevailing winds). Swash carries sediment up the beach at that angle. Backwash carries it straight back down (gravity). Net movement = sediment transported along the coast.<br><b>Groynes interrupt LSD:</b> they act as barriers perpendicular to the shore, trapping sediment on the updrift side — building the beach. Sediment cannot pass the groyne → less sediment reaches downdrift areas (sediment starvation).<br><b>At Sheringham:</b> LSD travels from <b>west to east</b> (evidenced by sediment piled on the west side of each groyne). Our orange experiment showed LSD velocity was <b>0.17 m/s</b> (protected) vs <b>0.55 m/s</b> (unprotected) — showing groynes do slow it.	Geography::Paper 3::Fieldwork - Sheringham
Basic	What is a constructive wave and what is a destructive wave?	<b>Constructive waves:</b> 7–10 waves per minute. Long wavelength, low height. Strong swash, weak backwash → deposit sediment, build beach.<br><b>Destructive waves:</b> 10–15 waves per minute. Short wavelength, high height. Weak swash, strong backwash → erode and remove sediment.<br><b>At Sheringham:</b> wave count used to determine type. Counted over 1 minute, repeated 3 times for reliability. Destructive waves are more common at Sheringham — hence the need for groynes to trap sediment the waves would otherwise remove.	Geography::Paper 3::Fieldwork - Sheringham
