Basic	What is the difference between series and parallel circuits?	Series: components connected in one loop. Same current through all. Total resistance = sum of individual resistances. Voltage shared. One breaks — all break. Parallel: components connected in separate branches. Same voltage across each branch. Total resistance less than smallest individual resistance. One breaks — others work.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Cloze	Power equations: P = {{c1::IV}} = {{c2::I²R}} = {{c3::V²/R}}. Units: {{c4::watts (W)}}. A 230V device drawing 2A: P = {{c5::460W}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Basic	What are the I-V characteristics of a resistor, filament lamp and diode?	Resistor: straight line through origin (constant resistance — Ohm's law). Filament lamp: curved — as current increases, temperature increases, resistance increases. Diode: only allows current in one direction — very high resistance in reverse direction.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Cloze	A thermistor's resistance {{c1::decreases}} as temperature increases. An LDR's resistance {{c2::decreases}} as light intensity increases. Both used in {{c3::sensing circuits}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Basic	What is the National Grid and why are transformers used?	The National Grid transmits electricity from power stations to homes and industry. Step-up transformers increase voltage (and decrease current) for transmission — reduces energy loss due to resistance in cables. Step-down transformers reduce voltage to safe levels for use (230V in homes).	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity

Basic	How do you calculate resistance in a series circuit?	<b>Series:</b> R_total = R₁ + R₂ + R₃... (resistances add up). Current same throughout. Voltage shared proportionally (larger R → larger V across it).<br><b>Example:</b> two 6Ω resistors in series: R_total = 12Ω.<br><b>Parallel (qualitative):</b> total resistance is less than the smallest individual resistor. Same voltage across each branch. Currents add up (I_total = I₁ + I₂). Note: calculating total resistance in parallel is not required.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Basic	What is the required practical for I-V characteristics? What graphs do you draw?	<b>Method:</b> set up circuit with ammeter in series, voltmeter in parallel with the component. Use variable resistor (rheostat) to change current. Record current and voltage for range of values. Reverse connections to get negative values.<br><b>Components tested:</b><br>• <b>Resistor:</b> straight line through origin → constant resistance (Ohm's Law).<br>• <b>Filament lamp:</b> S-curve (non-linear) → resistance increases as temperature increases.<br>• <b>Diode:</b> current only flows in one direction. Very high resistance in reverse direction → flat line.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
Basic	How do we stay safe from mains electricity? What is the role of the earth wire and fuse?	<b>Earth wire (green/yellow):</b> connects metal casing of appliance to earth (0V). If live wire touches casing → large current flows through earth wire → fuse blows → appliance isolated. Prevents electrocution.<br><b>Fuse:</b> thin wire that melts if too much current flows → breaks the circuit. Chosen to be slightly above normal operating current. E.g. 2A device → 3A fuse (NOT 5A — too slow to protect).<br><b>Circuit breaker:</b> automatically switches off if current exceeds threshold. Can be reset (unlike fuse which must be replaced). RCDs (residual current device) detect very small differences in current → trip in milliseconds.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Electricity
