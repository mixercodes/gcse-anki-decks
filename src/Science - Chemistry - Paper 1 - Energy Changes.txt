Basic	What is the difference between exothermic and endothermic reactions?	Exothermic: releases energy to surroundings — temperature rises. Products have less energy than reactants. Examples: combustion, neutralisation, respiration, hand warmers. Endothermic: absorbs energy from surroundings — temperature falls. Products have more energy. Examples: photosynthesis, thermal decomposition, sports injury packs.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Energy Changes
Basic	How do you interpret reaction profile (energy level) diagrams?	X-axis = reaction progress. Y-axis = energy. Reactants on left, products on right. Exothermic: products lower than reactants (overall energy released). Endothermic: products higher than reactants. Activation energy = energy needed to start the reaction (the 'hump'). A catalyst lowers activation energy.	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Energy Changes
Cloze	Bond energy calculations: energy is required to {{c1::break}} bonds (endothermic). Energy is released when bonds {{c2::form}} (exothermic). Overall energy change = energy to {{c3::break bonds}} − energy {{c4::released forming bonds}}. If result is negative, reaction is {{c5::exothermic}}.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Energy Changes
Basic	Worked example: calculate the energy change for H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl. Bond energies: H–H = 436 kJ/mol, Cl–Cl = 243 kJ/mol, H–Cl = 432 kJ/mol.	Energy to break bonds (in): H–H = 436, Cl–Cl = 243. Total in = 679 kJ/mol.<br>Energy released forming bonds (out): 2 × H–Cl = 2 × 432 = 864 kJ/mol.<br>Overall energy change = 679 − 864 = <b>−185 kJ/mol</b>.<br>Negative = <b>exothermic</b> (energy released to surroundings). Basic	What is the required practical for energy changes? What is measured and why use a polystyrene cup?	Mix two solutions (e.g. acid + alkali, or dissolving a salt) in a polystyrene cup. Record temperature of each solution before mixing. Mix and record maximum (or minimum for endothermic) temperature.<br><b>Polystyrene cup:</b> low thermal conductivity = less heat lost to surroundings = more accurate result.<br><b>Variables:</b> volume and concentration of solutions, starting temperature, mass of solid.<br><b>Worked example:</b> neutralisation — temperature rises (exothermic). Dissolving NH₄Cl — temperature falls (endothermic).	Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Energy Changes
Basic	How do you calculate the energy transferred in a calorimetry experiment using Q = mcΔT?	<b>Q = mcΔT</b><br>Q = energy transferred (J), m = mass of solution (g), c = specific heat capacity of water (4.2 J/g°C), ΔT = temperature change (°C).<br><b>Example:</b> 50 g of solution, temperature rises from 22°C to 35°C.<br>ΔT = 13°C → Q = 50 × 4.2 × 13 = <b>2730 J = 2.73 kJ</b>.<br>To find energy per mole: divide Q by moles of limiting reagent used. Basic	What are hydrogen fuel cells and what are their advantages and disadvantages?	A fuel cell uses hydrogen and oxygen to produce electricity directly via a chemical reaction: H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O.<br><b>Advantages:</b> only product is water (no CO₂ during use), more efficient than combustion engines, no moving parts, quiet.<br><b>Disadvantages:</b> hydrogen difficult to store and transport safely (explosive, low density), most hydrogen currently made from fossil fuels (so not truly zero carbon), infrastructure lacking, expensive to produce. Cloze	Activation energy: the {{c1::minimum energy}} required for particles to react when they collide. A {{c2::catalyst}} lowers the activation energy by providing an {{c3::alternative reaction pathway}}. On a reaction profile, a catalyst is shown as a {{c4::lower hump}} — the reactants and products are at the {{c5::same energy levels}} as without catalyst.		Science::Chemistry::Paper 1::Energy Changes
Basic	What is the difference between an exothermic and endothermic reaction on an energy profile diagram?	<b>Exothermic:</b> products are at a LOWER energy level than reactants. The difference (reactants − products) = energy released. Activation energy hump starts from reactants level.<br><b>Endothermic:</b> products are at a HIGHER energy level than reactants. Energy must be continuously supplied. Both show: reactants on left, activation energy peak, products on right.<br><b>Key mark scheme point:</b> the activation energy is measured from the reactants to the PEAK (top of hump), not from reactants to products. Basic	Why is the reaction between zinc and copper sulfate solution described as exothermic, and what would you observe?	Zinc displaces copper: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu.<br>It is exothermic — energy is released as new bonds (Zn–O in ZnSO₄) are stronger than bonds broken (Cu–O in CuSO₄).<br><b>Observations:</b> blue solution turns colourless/pale, orange/pink copper metal appears on zinc surface, temperature of solution increases.<br><b>Exam tip:</b> always state what you would measure (temperature with thermometer) and what change indicates exothermic (temperature rise).
