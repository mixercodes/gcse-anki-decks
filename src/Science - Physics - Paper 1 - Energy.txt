Basic	What are the main energy stores and transfers?	Stores: kinetic, gravitational potential, elastic potential, chemical, thermal, magnetic, electrostatic, nuclear. Transfers: mechanically (forces), electrically (current), by heating (temperature difference), by radiation (light/sound). Energy is conserved — total energy before = total energy after. Cloze	Kinetic energy: Ek = {{c1::½mv²}}. Where m = {{c2::mass (kg)}}, v = {{c3::speed (m/s)}}. E.g. 2kg object at 3m/s: Ek = 0.5 × 2 × 9 = {{c4::9 J}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Cloze	Gravitational potential energy: Ep = {{c1::mgh}}. Where m = mass (kg), g = {{c2::9.8 m/s²}} (gravitational field strength), h = {{c3::height (m)}}. E.g. 5kg object raised 10m: Ep = 5 × 9.8 × 10 = {{c4::490 J}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Cloze	Power = {{c1::energy transferred}} ÷ {{c2::time}}. Unit: {{c3::watts (W)}}. 1W = 1 J/s. A device with 60J in 3s: power = {{c4::20W}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Basic	What is the difference between renewable and non-renewable energy resources?	Non-renewable: fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) + nuclear — will run out, provide reliable baseload power, produce CO₂ (except nuclear). Renewable: solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, tidal, wave, biofuel — won't run out, often intermittent, smaller environmental footprint.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Cloze	Efficiency = {{c1::useful output energy}} ÷ {{c2::total input energy}} (× 100 for %). A device that transfers 80J usefully from 200J input: efficiency = {{c3::80/200 = 0.4 or 40%}}. Wasted energy goes to {{c4::thermal energy}} (heat).		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Basic	How is energy transferred in heating and what reduces unwanted transfer?	Conduction: vibrations passed between particles (solids). Convection: hot fluid rises, cool sinks — creates a current (fluids). Radiation: infrared electromagnetic waves — no medium needed. Reducing: insulation (traps air), double glazing, cavity walls, draught excluders, reflective foil.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Basic	What is a Sankey diagram and how do you interpret one?	A Sankey diagram represents energy transfers. The arrow width is proportional to the amount of energy.<br><b>Reading one:</b> input energy enters from the left (total width). Useful output goes straight ahead. Wasted energy (usually heat) branches off — often downwards.<br><b>Example:</b> a filament bulb with 100J input: 10J useful light (forward arrow), 90J waste heat (downward arrow).<br><b>Efficiency from Sankey:</b> useful output ÷ total input × 100 = 10/100 × 100 = 10% efficient.<br><b>Exam tip:</b> the wasted energy arrow should be wider than the useful arrow for most devices (most real processes are <50% efficient). Basic	How do you compare different energy resources? What are the advantages of each?	<b>Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas):</b> reliable, high energy density, existing infrastructure. Disadvantages: CO₂ (climate change), SO₂ (acid rain), finite.<br><b>Nuclear:</b> reliable, no CO₂ during generation, huge energy density. Disadvantages: radioactive waste, expensive to build/decommission, risk of accidents.<br><b>Wind:</b> renewable, no CO₂, cheap to run. Disadvantages: intermittent (needs wind), visual impact, noise, birds.<br><b>Solar:</b> renewable, no CO₂. Disadvantages: intermittent (day, weather), expensive panels.<br><b>Hydroelectric:</b> reliable, renewable, can store energy (pump at off-peak). Disadvantages: habitat destruction, flooding of land.<br><b>Biofuel:</b> renewable, carbon-neutral in theory. Disadvantages: land use competes with food.	Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
Cloze	Elastic potential energy: stored in stretched or compressed springs. Formula: Ep = {{c1::½ke²}}. k = {{c2::spring constant}} (N/m), e = {{c3::extension}} (m). A spring with k = 50 N/m stretched by 0.2 m: Ep = 0.5 × 50 × 0.04 = {{c4::1 J}}.		Science::Physics::Paper 1::Energy
