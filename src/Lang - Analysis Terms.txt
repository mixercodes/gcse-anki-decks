Adjective: describes a noun. Adverb: modifies a verb, adjective or other adverb. Pronoun: replaces a noun (I, we, you). Always name the word class when analysing — e.g. 'the verb X suggests...' 'the abstract noun X connotes...'	English Lang::Analysis Terms

Interrogative: asks a question — creates uncertainty, engages reader, challenges. Exclamatory: expresses strong emotion — creates urgency or passion. Imperative: gives a command — creates power and directness. Identifying the sentence type is a quick structural analysis point.	English Lang::Analysis Terms

Basic	What does 'zooming in' mean in language analysis and why does your teacher emphasise it?	Zooming in means selecting ONE specific word (ideally a single verb, noun or adjective) and unpacking its connotations in detail rather than commenting broadly. E.g. instead of 'the language is violent', zoom in: 'the verb unseamed has connotations of surgical precision, suggesting Macbeth's violence is controlled and purposeful rather than chaotic.' The more specific the word class and the richer the connotations, the higher the mark.	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	What is the difference between connotation and denotation?	Denotation: the literal, dictionary definition of a word. Connotation: the associations, feelings or ideas a word carries beyond its literal meaning. In analysis you always want connotations. E.g. 'red' denotation = a colour. Connotations = danger, blood, passion, warning. Good analysis goes beyond what a word means to what it suggests.	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	What are the three levels of analysis for any quotation?	1. Identify the technique/word class — name it precisely. 2. Zoom in on the specific word — what does it literally mean? 3. Expand to connotations/effect — what does it suggest? What does it make the reader feel? Link to the writer's intent/context. Never stop at level 1 (identifying). A grade 9 response lives at level 3.	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Cloze	{{c1::Cyclical structure}}: when the {{c2::ending mirrors or echoes the opening}}, creating a sense of completion or irony. E.g. Macbeth opens and closes with battle and a rightful king — the {{c3::natural order is restored}}.		English Lang::Analysis Terms
Cloze	{{c1::Iambic pentameter}}: the 'natural' rhythm of English speech — da-DUM × 5 per line. When characters break from it, something is {{c2::wrong or disordered}}. The witches speak in {{c3::trochaic tetrameter}} (opposite rhythm) — marking them as {{c4::unnatural}}.		English Lang::Analysis Terms
Cloze	{{c1::Rhyming couplet}}: two consecutive lines that rhyme. In Shakespeare, used to {{c2::signal the end of a scene}} or emphasise a character's resolution. E.g. 'Stars, hide your fires / Let not light see my black and deep {{c3::desires}}'.		English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	How do you approach an unseen poem — what three things must you always analyse?	1. Language — zoom in on specific word choices (verbs, nouns, adjectives), name techniques (metaphor, sibilance, plosive etc.), explore connotations. 2. Structure — form (sonnet, free verse, dramatic monologue), stanza lengths, enjambment, caesura, where the volta falls, what the opening and closing images are. 3. Context — who is the poet? When did they write? What might their purpose be? Why does this matter for the poem's meaning? Always link everything to effect on the reader.	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	What is the difference between a 24-mark and an 8-mark unseen poetry question?	24-mark (Q1): detailed analysis of ONE poem. Language, structure, form, effect. Several developed points with close focus on specific words. 8-mark (Q2): COMPARISON of both poems. Focus on differences AND similarities in how both poets present the theme. Two developed comparative points, 1:1 evidence ratio, use comparative connectives (whereas, similarly, in contrast, unlike).	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	What makes a quotation worth analysing in poetry?	Choose quotations that are: short (one to five words ideally), contain a specific technique, carry rich connotations, and connect to the poem's central argument. Avoid long quotations — they waste time and the analysis becomes vague. The best quotes are single powerful words: a verb, a noun, an unexpected adjective. These give you the most to say. Basic	What is the difference between caesura, enjambment and volta?	Caesura: a pause within a line (often marked by punctuation) — creates tension or emphasis. Enjambment: a sentence runs over from one line to the next without a pause — creates flow, urgency or a sense of things not ending. Volta: the turning point of a poem where tone/argument shifts — often signals a change in understanding.	English Lang::Language Skills
Basic	How should you approach an unseen poetry question?	Always analyse: Language (word choice, techniques, imagery), Structure (form, enjambment, stanzas, volta, rhyme scheme), and Context (poet's background, when written, why). Link EVERYTHING to the effect on the reader. For a comparison question: track through chronologically, maintain balance of evidence between poems, combine comparisons rather than writing separately. Basic	What is the difference between tone and mood in poetry analysis?	Tone: the writer's or speaker's attitude towards their subject (e.g. bitter, elegiac, ironic, reverent). Mood: the emotional atmosphere created in the reader (e.g. unsettling, melancholy, claustrophobic). A poem can have a bitter tone but create a melancholic mood. Specify which you mean: 'the tone is... while the mood it creates is...'	English Lang::Analysis Terms
Basic	What does CHRIS PRICE stand for and why use it?	CHRIS PRICE is a toolkit of analytical verbs for zooming in on language. C — Conveys. H — Highlights. R — Reinforces. I — Infers. S — Suggests. P — Portrays. R — Refers. I — Implies. C — Connotes. E — Evokes. Using these instead of 'shows' or 'makes' instantly elevates the precision of your analysis — e.g. 'the verb X connotes...' or 'the adjective X evokes a sense of...' Each verb carries a slightly different weight: 'connotes' is specific to word-level analysis; 'reinforces' implies something is building across the text; 'evokes' brings in sensory or emotional response. Basic	What is the difference between a monologue and a dramatic monologue?	Monologue: one character speaks at length (may address other characters on stage). Dramatic monologue: a specific poetic form — one speaker addresses an implied, silent listener; the reader infers the situation. Crucially, the speaker often reveals more than they intend — the gap between what is said and what is implied is the point. E.g. My Last Duchess: the Duke addresses a visitor but unconsciously reveals his guilt.	English Lang::Analysis Terms
